# Email Subject Line Generator

## Purpose

Generate high-open-rate subject lines tailored to the user's ICP and offer. The subject line is the single most important element of a cold email — if it doesn't get opened, nothing else matters. This skill produces 25+ personalized subject lines using proven psychological triggers, organized by formula type, with A/B test pairs ready to deploy.

---

## The Science of Subject Lines

### Why Open Rate Is the First Gate

```
COLD EMAIL FUNNEL:
100 emails sent
  → [SUBJECT LINE GATE] → 40-50 opened (40-50% open rate = strong)
    → [BODY COPY GATE] → 4-8 replies (8-15% reply rate of opens = strong)
      → [CTA GATE] → 2-4 meetings (50% meeting rate from positive replies)

If your subject line only gets 15% opens instead of 45%:
100 emails → 15 opened → 1-2 replies → 0-1 meetings

The subject line 3x'd your pipeline without changing anything else.
```

### Open Rate Benchmarks by Context

```
Cold email (no prior relationship):
- Excellent: 50%+
- Good: 35-50%
- Average: 25-35%
- Poor: 15-25%
- Terrible: <15% (deliverability issue or bad subject lines)

Warm email (prior interaction):
- Excellent: 60%+
- Good: 45-60%
- Average: 35-45%

Re-engagement email:
- Excellent: 40%+
- Good: 25-40%
- Average: 15-25%

NOTE: Open rate tracking has become less reliable since Apple's Mail Privacy
Protection (iOS 15+). Use reply rate as a more dependable secondary metric.
Open rates may appear inflated due to privacy pre-fetching.
```

## Subject Line Fundamentals

### Optimal Length

```
CHARACTERS:
- Sweet spot: 28-50 characters (5-8 words)
- Maximum before truncation on desktop: ~60 characters
- Maximum before truncation on mobile: ~35-40 characters
- Shorter is almost always better for cold email

MOBILE CONSIDERATION:
- 60%+ of emails are first seen on mobile
- iPhone shows ~35 characters in portrait mode
- Android varies by device/app, but ~40 characters is safe
- Front-load the important words — put the hook in the first 4-5 words

EXAMPLES BY LENGTH:
Too short (suspicious): "hi" (2 chars) — feels like spam
Too long: "I wanted to reach out about a potential opportunity for your team at [COMPANY]" (78 chars) — gets cut off, boring
Just right: "question about [COMPANY]'s growth plan" (37 chars) — curiosity, specific, fits mobile
```

### Capitalization Rules

```
DO: Use sentence case (capitalize first word only)
    "quick question about your pipeline"
    → Feels natural, like a real email from a real person

DO: Use all lowercase (for casual/personal feel)
    "thoughts on this?"
    → Feels like a message from a friend or colleague

DON'T: Use Title Case For Every Word
    "Quick Question About Your Pipeline"
    → Feels formal and promotional

DON'T: USE ALL CAPS
    "QUICK QUESTION ABOUT YOUR PIPELINE"
    → Spam trigger, screams amateur

DON'T: Use Excessive Punctuation!!!
    → One question mark is fine. Three exclamation points = spam filter.
```

## The 12 Subject Line Formulas

### Formula 1: The Question

**Psychology:** Questions create an open loop in the brain that demands closure. The prospect feels compelled to open the email to close the loop.

**When to use:** When you can ask something genuinely relevant to their role or situation. Works for almost any ICP.

**Template:** `[NAME], quick question about [specific thing]?`

**Examples across industries:**

```
For SaaS founders:
- "[NAME], quick question about your onboarding flow?"
- "how are you handling [SPECIFIC CHALLENGE] right now?"
- "question about [COMPANY]'s approach to [TOPIC]"

For Marketing leaders:
- "[NAME], curious about your attribution model?"
- "question about [COMPANY]'s content strategy"
- "how is [COMPANY] handling lead quality?"

For Sales leaders:
- "what's your team's follow-up cadence look like?"
- "[NAME], question about your outbound process"
- "how does [COMPANY] handle pipeline forecasting?"

For Operations/Finance:
- "question about [COMPANY]'s vendor evaluation process"
- "how is [COMPANY] handling [OPERATIONAL CHALLENGE]?"
- "[NAME], quick question about your [PROCESS]"

For Agency owners:
- "question about how [COMPANY] handles [SERVICE AREA]"
- "how are you scaling [SPECIFIC FUNCTION]?"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 35-55%

---

### Formula 2: The Mutual Connection

**Psychology:** Social proof and trust transfer. If someone they know suggested this, it moves from cold to warm instantly.

**When to use:** ONLY when you genuinely have a mutual connection. Fabricating this destroys trust. Can also reference a shared group, community, or event.

**Template:** `[MUTUAL CONNECTION] suggested I reach out`

**Examples:**

```
- "[CONTACT NAME] mentioned you might be interested in this"
- "[CONTACT NAME] suggested we connect"
- "following up on [CONTACT NAME]'s recommendation"
- "we're both in [GROUP/COMMUNITY] — quick question"
- "[CONTACT NAME] thought you'd find this useful"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 45-65% (highest of any formula when genuine)

---

### Formula 3: The Observation

**Psychology:** Shows you've done research and this isn't a spray-and-pray email. Flattering (you noticed them specifically). Creates curiosity about what you observed.

**When to use:** When you can reference something specific and genuine — their website, a recent post, their product, their hiring activity, their content.

**Template:** `noticed [specific thing about their company]`

**Examples:**

```
- "noticed [COMPANY] is expanding into [MARKET]"
- "saw your recent post about [TOPIC]"
- "noticed [COMPANY] is hiring for [ROLE] — idea for you"
- "your [SPECIFIC PAGE/FEATURE] caught my eye"
- "saw [COMPANY]'s announcement about [NEWS]"
- "noticed something about [COMPANY]'s [SPECIFIC AREA]"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 40-55%

---

### Formula 4: The Result

**Psychology:** Numbers are attention magnets. Specific results create credibility and curiosity ("How did they do that? Could I get that too?").

**When to use:** When you have concrete, quantifiable results from a client similar to the prospect. The more specific and impressive, the better.

**Template:** `[number]% [improvement] for [similar company type]`

**Examples:**

```
- "how [SIMILAR COMPANY] cut [METRIC] by 40%"
- "[X]% increase in [METRIC] — relevant for [COMPANY]?"
- "[SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE] went from [A] to [B] in [TIMEFRAME]"
- "3x pipeline growth for a [SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE]"
- "[SIMILAR COMPANY] saved [X] hours/week on [TASK]"
- "from [OLD METRIC] to [NEW METRIC] in [TIMEFRAME]"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 35-50%

---

### Formula 5: The Curiosity Gap

**Psychology:** Creates intrigue by suggesting a connection between two things without explaining it. The brain needs to open the email to understand the connection.

**When to use:** When you want to be intriguing without being clickbaity. Works well when there's a genuine synergy to discuss.

**Template:** `[their company] + [your company/approach]`

**Examples:**

```
- "[COMPANY] + [YOUR SERVICE CATEGORY]"
- "[COMPANY]'s [INITIATIVE] + an idea"
- "a thought on [COMPANY] + [TREND]"
- "[THEIR INDUSTRY] + [YOUR APPROACH] = [RESULT]?"
- "[COMPANY] + [COMPETITOR OF THEIRS] insight"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 35-50%

---

### Formula 6: The Direct

**Psychology:** Respects the prospect's time. No games, no tricks — just a clear statement of what this is about. Works especially well with senior executives who hate fluff.

**When to use:** When your prospect values directness (C-suite, technical roles, experienced buyers). When the problem is well-known and doesn't need a clever hook.

**Template:** `[specific problem] at [their company]`

**Examples:**

```
- "[SPECIFIC CHALLENGE] at [COMPANY]"
- "reducing [PAIN POINT] for [COMPANY]"
- "[COMPANY]'s [METRIC] — can we help?"
- "solving [PROBLEM] for [INDUSTRY] companies"
- "idea for [COMPANY]'s [SPECIFIC AREA]"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 30-45%

---

### Formula 7: The Resource

**Psychology:** Leads with value, not a pitch. The prospect is getting something useful regardless of whether they buy. Lower threat, higher open rate.

**When to use:** When you have a genuinely useful resource — a guide, a benchmark report, a tool, an audit, a checklist. Not a brochure or sales deck.

**Template:** `resource for [their specific challenge]`

**Examples:**

```
- "[TOPIC] playbook for [THEIR ROLE/INDUSTRY]"
- "benchmark data on [THEIR CHALLENGE]"
- "free [AUDIT/ASSESSMENT/TOOL] for [COMPANY]"
- "[INDUSTRY] report — thought you'd want this"
- "checklist: [SPECIFIC PROCESS THEY CARE ABOUT]"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 30-45%

---

### Formula 8: The Timely

**Psychology:** Leverages recency and relevance. When tied to a real event, it proves you're paying attention and creates natural urgency.

**When to use:** When there's a genuine trigger event — funding announcement, new hire, product launch, acquisition, regulatory change, earnings report, conference.

**Template:** `re: [recent event/news about them]`

**Examples:**

```
- "congrats on the [FUNDING ROUND/MILESTONE]"
- "re: [COMPANY]'s [RECENT ANNOUNCEMENT]"
- "saw the news about [SPECIFIC EVENT] — thought of you"
- "after [INDUSTRY EVENT], a thought for [COMPANY]"
- "since [COMPANY]'s [CHANGE], this might help"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 40-60% (high when trigger is genuine and recent)

---

### Formula 9: The Short and Cryptic

**Psychology:** Extreme brevity creates maximum curiosity. Looks like an internal email or a message from someone they know. Very high open rate but must deliver value in the body to avoid frustration.

**When to use:** As a change of pace in sequences (especially emails 4-6). When you want to cut through inbox noise with something that doesn't look like a cold email at all.

**Template:** 1-3 words, lowercase, no punctuation or minimal

**Examples:**

```
- "quick thought"
- "idea for you"
- "[COMPANY NAME]"
- "[NAME]?"
- "this relevant?"
- "had a thought"
- "one more thing"
- "worth it?"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 45-65% (very high open rate, but watch reply rate — if the body doesn't deliver, it's just clickbait)

---

### Formula 10: The Personal

**Psychology:** Uses their name and implies this was written specifically for them. In an inbox full of promotional emails, a personally addressed subject line stands out.

**When to use:** When you have something genuinely specific to say to this person. Combining their name with a specific value proposition is powerful.

**Template:** `for [NAME] — [specific value]`

**Examples:**

```
- "for [NAME] — [SPECIFIC RESOURCE/INSIGHT]"
- "[NAME], this is for you"
- "[NAME] — saw this and thought of [COMPANY]"
- "specifically for [NAME]'s team"
- "[NAME] — [SPECIFIC RESULT] idea"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 35-50%

---

### Formula 11: The Social Proof

**Psychology:** Name-dropping a company similar to theirs creates instant credibility and peer pressure. "If my competitor/peer is doing this, maybe I should pay attention."

**When to use:** When you have a recognizable client in their industry or peer group. The named company should be someone they'd know and respect — not necessarily a household name.

**Template:** `how [similar company] solved [problem]`

**Examples:**

```
- "how [SIMILAR COMPANY] handles [CHALLENGE]"
- "what [SIMILAR COMPANY] did about [PROBLEM]"
- "[SIMILAR COMPANY]'s approach to [TOPIC] — relevant?"
- "the [METHOD] that [SIMILAR COMPANY] used for [RESULT]"
- "[SIMILAR COMPANY]'s [METRIC] before vs. after"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 35-50%

---

### Formula 12: The Follow-Up

**Psychology:** These are for emails 2+ in a sequence. They leverage the existing thread (or the memory of it) and create gentle urgency. The "re:" prefix is recognized as a continuation.

**When to use:** For emails 2-7 in a sequence. Vary between in-thread replies (which carry the original subject) and new threads with follow-up subject lines.

**Template:** Varies by stage in sequence

**Examples:**

```
Early follow-up (email 2-3):
- "re: my last email"
- "one more thought on [TOPIC]"
- "forgot to mention this"
- "adding to my previous note"

Mid-sequence (email 4-5):
- "[NAME]?"
- "any thoughts?"
- "worth revisiting?"
- "different angle on [TOPIC]"

Late sequence / breakup (email 6-7):
- "should I close the loop?"
- "closing the file on this"
- "last attempt — then I'll stop"
- "permission to close your file?"
```

**Expected open rate range:** 30-50% (later emails naturally get lower opens, but good follow-up subjects maintain engagement)

## What Kills Open Rates

### Comprehensive List of Subject Line Mistakes

```
FORMATTING ERRORS:
- ALL CAPS (even partial: "FREE Consultation for Your Team")
- Excessive punctuation (!!!, ???, !!??)
- Emojis in cold email (acceptable in marketing email, risky in cold outreach)
- Very long subject lines (60+ characters — gets truncated, looks mass-sent)
- Starting with "RE:" or "FW:" when it's not a genuine reply/forward

SPAM TRIGGER WORDS AND PHRASES:
- "Free," "100% free," "no cost," "complimentary"
- "Act now," "limited time," "don't miss," "last chance"
- "Click here," "open immediately"
- "You've been selected," "congratulations," "you're a winner"
- "Make money," "earn $," "income opportunity," "double your"
- "No obligation," "no purchase necessary," "risk free"
- "Dear friend," "Dear Sir/Madam," "To whom it may concern"
- "Guaranteed," "promise," "100%"

TRUST BREAKERS:
- Misleading subjects that don't match the email content
- Subjects that imply urgency when there isn't any
- Subjects that pretend to be personal when the email is clearly mass-sent
- Using the prospect's full name in the subject (feels database-pulled)
- Referencing connections that don't exist

BORING/GENERIC SUBJECTS:
- "Introduction" or "Introducing [COMPANY]"
- "Partnership opportunity"
- "Quick question" (overused — add specificity)
- "Reaching out" or "Just reaching out"
- "Checking in" (says nothing about value)
- "Can I have 5 minutes of your time?"
- "[YOUR COMPANY] + [THEIR COMPANY]" (without context)
```

## Preview Text Strategy

### What Is Preview Text?

```
Preview text (also called preheader text) is the 40-90 characters that appear
after the subject line in the inbox preview. In most email clients, if you don't
set preview text intentionally, the first line of your email body shows up.

WHAT THE PROSPECT SEES:
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ From: [Your Name]                                        │
│ Subject: question about [COMPANY]'s pipeline             │
│ Preview: saw you're hiring 3 SDRs — I might be able to.. │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

The subject line gets them curious.
The preview text seals the open.
```

### Preview Text Best Practices

```
1. The preview text should COMPLEMENT the subject, not repeat it
   BAD: Subject "question about hiring" → Preview "I have a question about hiring..."
   GOOD: Subject "question about hiring" → Preview "noticed 5 open roles on your careers page..."

2. Use the preview text to add specificity that wouldn't fit in the subject
   Subject: "[X]% improvement for [SIMILAR COMPANY]"
   Preview: "they were struggling with the same [CHALLENGE] you mentioned in..."

3. Start your email body with a strong personalized first line
   Since preview text is often the first line of the email, a strong opening
   line serves double duty — it's both preview text and the email hook.

4. Keep it conversational — preview text that sounds like a real email
   (not a marketing blast) drives higher opens.

5. Avoid starting your email with "Hi [NAME]," because that becomes your
   preview text and wastes valuable real estate. Instead:
   BAD first line as preview: "Hi Sarah, I hope this email finds you..."
   GOOD first line as preview: "Noticed [COMPANY] just launched [PRODUCT]..."
```

## A/B Testing Subject Lines

### How to Structure a Subject Line Test

```
STEP 1: Create two subject lines that test ONE variable
  - Same formula, different wording
  - OR different formulas targeting the same pain

  Example A/B pair:
  A: "question about [COMPANY]'s pipeline process"  (Question formula)
  B: "how [SIMILAR COMPANY] fixed their pipeline"   (Social Proof formula)

STEP 2: Split your list randomly 50/50
  - Do NOT split by any other criteria (company size, industry)
  - Random split ensures the only variable is the subject line

STEP 3: Send at the same time on the same day
  - Eliminates time-of-day/day-of-week as a variable

STEP 4: Wait for at least 100 sends per variant
  - Minimum 100 per variant to approach statistical significance
  - 200+ per variant is more reliable
  - Don't call a winner after 20 sends — the data is too noisy

STEP 5: Measure open rate AND reply rate
  - A subject line with 50% open rate but 1% reply rate lost to one with
    35% open rate and 5% reply rate
  - Open rate is the primary metric for subject line testing
  - But always check if higher opens translate to higher replies

STEP 6: Implement the winner and test the next pair
  - Use the winner as your new control
  - Create a new variant to challenge it
  - Continuous improvement
```

### 10 A/B Test Pairs Ready to Deploy

```
Test 1 — Question vs. Result:
A: "[NAME], how are you handling [CHALLENGE]?"
B: "[X]% [IMPROVEMENT] for companies like [COMPANY]"

Test 2 — Short/Cryptic vs. Specific:
A: "quick thought"
B: "idea for [COMPANY]'s [SPECIFIC AREA]"

Test 3 — Name in subject vs. No name:
A: "[NAME], noticed something about [COMPANY]"
B: "noticed something about [COMPANY]"

Test 4 — Lowercase vs. Sentence case:
A: "question about [company]'s growth plan"
B: "Question about [COMPANY]'s growth plan"

Test 5 — Company name vs. Generic:
A: "[COMPANY]'s [CHALLENGE]"
B: "[INDUSTRY] [CHALLENGE]"

Test 6 — Peer reference vs. Direct:
A: "how [SIMILAR COMPANY] solved [PROBLEM]"
B: "solving [PROBLEM] at [COMPANY]"

Test 7 — Curiosity vs. Value:
A: "[COMPANY] + [YOUR SERVICE AREA]"
B: "resource for [COMPANY]'s [CHALLENGE]"

Test 8 — Timely vs. Evergreen:
A: "re: [RECENT NEWS ABOUT COMPANY]"
B: "thought on [COMPANY]'s [ONGOING INITIATIVE]"

Test 9 — Question mark vs. No punctuation:
A: "worth exploring this for [COMPANY]?"
B: "worth exploring this for [COMPANY]"

Test 10 — Follow-up styles:
A: "re: my last note"
B: "[NAME]?"
```

## Subject Line Testing Tools

```
1. Send to seed accounts and check inbox placement
   - Free, manual method
   - Shows you exactly how the subject renders in Gmail, Outlook, etc.

2. mail-tester.com
   - Checks for spam trigger words in subject line and body
   - Score out of 10 includes subject line assessment

3. SubjectLine.com
   - Free subject line scoring tool
   - Grades on length, word choice, and sentiment
   - Take scores with a grain of salt — test with real sends

4. CoSchedule Headline Analyzer
   - Originally for blog headlines but useful for subject lines
   - Scores emotional impact, word balance, and length

5. Your own data
   - The best testing tool is your own campaign data
   - Track open rates by subject line formula and refine over time
   - Build a swipe file of your top-performing subject lines
```

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering the subject line library, verify:

- [ ] At least 25 unique subject lines have been generated
- [ ] Subject lines are organized by formula type
- [ ] At least 5 A/B test pairs are included with the variable being tested clearly labeled
- [ ] All subject lines are under 50 characters (check each one)
- [ ] No spam trigger words are present in any subject line
- [ ] Subject lines use sentence case or lowercase (no Title Case or ALL CAPS)
- [ ] Subject lines are personalized to the user's specific ICP and offer (not generic)
- [ ] Follow-up subject lines are provided for the full sequence (emails 2-7)
- [ ] Each subject line would feel natural coming from a real person (not a marketing blast)
- [ ] Preview text strategy is addressed — first lines of email templates complement subject lines
- [ ] Subject lines avoid misleading or clickbait framing
- [ ] Variety is present — not all subject lines follow the same formula
- [ ] Mobile preview length has been considered (first 35 characters carry the hook)
