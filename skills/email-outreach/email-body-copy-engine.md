# Email Body Copy Engine

## Purpose

Write cold email body copy that generates replies. Not opens. Not reads. Replies. This skill produces complete email templates using 8 proven copywriting frameworks, personalized to the user's ICP and offer, with variations for different personalization tiers and scenarios.

---

## Core Principle: Reply, Don't Read

```
A cold email is NOT:
- A newsletter
- A product brochure
- A blog post
- An essay about your company
- A list of your features and achievements

A cold email IS:
- A conversation starter
- A one-to-one message that happens to be sent at scale
- A vehicle to earn 15 seconds of attention and convert it to a reply

THE TEST: If your email could be published as a LinkedIn post or put on a
website, it's too long, too informational, and too impersonal. Rewrite it.

A good cold email should feel like a message you'd send to a colleague
you haven't talked to in a while — brief, specific, and to the point.
```

## Ideal Email Length

### The Data on Length

```
COLD EMAIL LENGTH BENCHMARKS:
- 50-75 words:  Highest reply rates (sweet spot for first touch)
- 75-125 words: Strong reply rates (good for emails with case studies)
- 125-175 words: Acceptable (but starting to lose people)
- 175-250 words: Below average reply rates
- 250+ words:   Significantly lower reply rates (too long for cold email)

RULE OF THUMB: Your email should be readable in under 10 seconds.
If it takes longer, it's too long.

WHY SHORTER WINS:
1. Busy people scan, they don't read
2. Long emails signal "this person wants something from me"
3. Short emails signal "this person respects my time"
4. On mobile (where 60%+ of emails are first seen), long emails
   require scrolling — most people won't
5. Short emails look like personal messages, not marketing
```

### Word Count by Email Position in Sequence

```
Email 1 (Opener):        50-100 words — Get attention, establish relevance
Email 2 (Value Add):     75-125 words — More room for a resource or insight
Email 3 (Social Proof):  75-125 words — Case study needs a bit more space
Email 4 (Different Angle): 50-100 words — Fresh take, keep it punchy
Email 5 (Question):      30-60 words — Ultra-short, just a question
Email 6 (Direct Ask):    50-75 words — Clear and direct
Email 7 (Breakup):       30-50 words — The shortest email in the sequence
```

## The Anatomy of a High-Converting Cold Email

### The 5-Part Structure

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. PERSONALIZED OPENING LINE            │
│    (1 sentence — shows you did research) │
├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 2. PROBLEM / OBSERVATION                │
│    (1-2 sentences — why you're reaching │
│    out, what you noticed, what problem  │
│    you can solve)                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 3. SOLUTION TEASE                       │
│    (1 sentence — what you do, framed    │
│    as a result, not a feature)          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 4. SOCIAL PROOF (optional)              │
│    (1 sentence — a specific result for  │
│    a similar company)                   │
├─────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 5. CTA                                  │
│    (1 sentence — one clear next step)   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────┘

TOTAL: 5-7 sentences. 50-125 words. That's it.
```

## The 8 Email Copy Frameworks

### Framework 1: Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

**How it works:** State their problem. Twist the knife (make the problem feel more painful). Offer the solution.

**When to use:** When the pain point is well-known and acute. When the prospect is likely already frustrated by this problem.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

Most [ICP TITLE]s at [COMPANY SIZE] companies struggle with [SPECIFIC PROBLEM].
It usually means [PAINFUL CONSEQUENCE] — and that leads to [BIGGER PAIN].

We help [ICP DESCRIPTION] [SPECIFIC OUTCOME] without [COMMON OBJECTION].
[SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE] saw [SPECIFIC RESULT] in [TIMEFRAME].

Worth a quick chat to see if this applies to [COMPANY]?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — IT Services for mid-market:**

```
Noticed [COMPANY] is hiring two new developers — congrats on the growth.

Most mid-market engineering leaders tell us that onboarding new devs takes
3-6 months before they're fully productive. Meanwhile, the team's velocity
drops because senior devs are pulled into mentoring.

We help engineering teams cut onboarding time by 60% with structured
ramp-up systems. [SIMILAR COMPANY] had new hires shipping code in week 3.

Would this be worth 15 minutes to explore?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Financial consulting for agencies:**

```
Saw [COMPANY]'s work on the [CLIENT/PROJECT] — impressive stuff.

Most agency owners I talk to are growing revenue but watching their
margins shrink. More clients, more staff, more overhead — but profit
stays flat or even declines.

We help agencies identify the profit leaks and restructure pricing so
growth actually translates to profit. One agency went from 12% to 28%
net margin in one quarter.

Mind if I share how? 15-minute call, no pitch.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 5-10% (Tier 2 personalization)

---

### Framework 2: Before-After-Bridge (BAB)

**How it works:** Paint their current reality (Before). Paint the desired future (After). Position yourself as the Bridge between the two.

**When to use:** When the desired outcome is aspirational and emotionally compelling. When the gap between current state and desired state is large.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

Right now, most [ICP TITLE]s at [INDUSTRY] companies are [CURRENT STATE
— describing their frustrating reality].

Imagine if instead, [DESIRED STATE — describing the outcome they want,
in specific terms].

That's exactly what we helped [SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE] achieve —
[SPECIFIC RESULT] in [TIMEFRAME].

Open to hearing how?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Marketing automation for e-commerce:**

```
Noticed [COMPANY] just launched a new product line — exciting.

Right now, most e-commerce marketing leads are manually segmenting
audiences and guessing which campaigns to send to whom.

Imagine if your top 20% of customers automatically got VIP treatment,
lapsed buyers got re-engaged with the right offer, and your team
spent zero time on manual segmentation.

We helped a [SIMILAR SIZE] DTC brand increase email revenue 3.2x
by building exactly that system.

Worth a 15-minute look?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 4-8%

---

### Framework 3: Question-Based

**How it works:** Open with a question that forces them to think "yes, that's me" or "yes, that's my problem." Once they agree internally, they're primed to keep reading.

**When to use:** When you can identify a question that nearly every person in this ICP segment would answer "yes" to. The question must be specific to their role, not generic.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

[QUESTION THAT RESONATES WITH THEIR SITUATION]?

[1-2 SENTENCES ABOUT HOW YOU SOLVE IT, WITH A SPECIFIC RESULT]

[CTA]

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Sales training for B2B companies:**

```
[NAME], noticed [COMPANY]'s sales team has grown to 15+ reps.

Quick question — are your newest reps ramping as fast as your
top performers did?

We help [COMPANY SIZE] sales orgs build repeatable onboarding
playbooks. Last client cut ramp time from 6 months to 8 weeks
and saw new reps hit quota 3x faster.

Worth exploring?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Cybersecurity for healthcare:**

```
Saw [COMPANY]'s expansion into telehealth — great move.

If you got hit with a ransomware attack this week, how quickly
could your team recover patient data without paying?

We help healthcare companies build resilient backup and recovery
systems. One health network avoided a $2M ransom payment because
of the system we built.

Would a 15-minute overview be useful?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 5-12% (questions drive high engagement)

### Framework 4: Case Study Lead

**How it works:** Lead immediately with a specific result. No throat-clearing, no setup — just "here's what happened for someone like you."

**When to use:** When you have a strong, quantifiable case study that's directly relevant to the prospect's situation. The case study company should be in their industry or peer group.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

[SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE] was [THEIR PREVIOUS SITUATION].
After [TIME/ENGAGEMENT], they [SPECIFIC RESULT].

The main thing that changed: [ONE KEY INSIGHT OR ACTION].

We've done this for [X] companies in [THEIR INDUSTRY].

Want me to share the specifics? Takes 15 minutes.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Recruitment for tech companies:**

```
Saw [COMPANY] has 12 open roles on the careers page right now.

A [SIMILAR SIZE] SaaS company came to us after spending 4 months
trying to fill 8 engineering roles. We filled all 8 in 6 weeks,
and the average hire quality score was 9.1/10 from hiring managers.

The key difference: we targeted passive candidates using a
proprietary sourcing method instead of relying on job boards.

Worth a quick call to see if we can help [COMPANY] the same way?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 6-12%

---

### Framework 5: Observation-Based

**How it works:** Reference something specific you noticed about their company, then connect it to a suggestion or opportunity.

**When to use:** When you can do genuine research and find something meaningful — not a generic observation. This framework requires the most research but produces the best results.

**Template:**

```
[SPECIFIC OBSERVATION ABOUT THEIR COMPANY — what you noticed and where]

[WHY IT MATTERS / WHAT IT MIGHT MEAN FOR THEM]

[HOW YOU'VE HELPED SIMILAR COMPANIES IN THIS EXACT SITUATION]

[SOFT CTA — would it be helpful to discuss this?]

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — SEO/Content for B2B SaaS:**

```
I was looking at [COMPANY]'s blog and noticed you're publishing
consistently but most posts aren't ranking on page 1 for their
target keywords.

In my experience, that usually means the content is good but the
SEO architecture (internal linking, keyword clustering, technical
foundation) needs work.

We helped [SIMILAR COMPANY] go from 500 to 12,000 monthly organic
visitors in 6 months by restructuring their content strategy
without publishing more.

Would a quick audit of [COMPANY]'s content be useful? I can put
one together in a few days.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 8-15% (highest when observation is genuinely insightful)

---

### Framework 6: Permission-Based

**How it works:** Ask permission before pitching. This lowers the threat level and makes the prospect feel in control. When people feel in control, they're more receptive.

**When to use:** For senior executives, enterprise targets, or anyone who likely gets a lot of cold outreach. The "ask permission" approach feels more respectful.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

I have an idea for how [COMPANY] could [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]
based on [WHAT YOU'VE SEEN/KNOW].

Mind if I share it? Takes 2 minutes to read.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Operations consulting for logistics:**

```
Noticed [COMPANY] expanded to 3 new distribution centers this year.

I have a thought on how you might reduce last-mile delivery
costs by 15-20% without changing carriers — based on what we've
seen work at [X] other logistics companies at your scale.

Mind if I share the approach? Quick read, no strings.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 6-10% (strong for C-suite)

---

### Framework 7: Value-First

**How it works:** Give an actionable insight, tip, or resource upfront — no strings attached. Don't ask for anything in the first email. Let the value earn the reply.

**When to use:** When you have genuine expertise to share. For building relationships with high-value targets. For long sales cycles where trust matters more than speed.

**Template:**

```
[PERSONALIZED FIRST LINE]

[ACTIONABLE INSIGHT OR TIP RELEVANT TO THEIR SITUATION]

[WHY THIS MATTERS / THE IMPACT IF THEY ACT ON IT]

No pitch — just thought this might be useful for [COMPANY].

Happy to share more if this resonates.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Data analytics for retail:**

```
[NAME], was looking at [COMPANY]'s online store and noticed
your product page load time is averaging 4.2 seconds.

Industry data shows that every 1-second improvement in page speed
increases conversion rate by 7%. For a store at your traffic level,
that could mean an extra $[ESTIMATED AMOUNT] per month.

Not trying to sell you anything — we work in this space and I
thought you'd want to know. If you want, I can share two quick
fixes that usually knock 1-2 seconds off immediately.

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 5-8% (lower immediate conversion but builds long-term pipeline)

---

### Framework 8: The Straight Shooter

**How it works:** No fluff, no clever hooks, no storytelling. Just: "Here's what I do. Here's a result. Interested?"

**When to use:** When your prospect is no-nonsense (engineers, finance people, founders). When your offer is straightforward and the value proposition is self-evident. Late in a sequence when you've already tried softer approaches.

**Template:**

```
Hi [NAME],

I help [ICP DESCRIPTION] [SPECIFIC OUTCOME].

[ONE RESULT: COMPANY TYPE + METRIC + TIMEFRAME]

Worth 15 minutes to see if we can do the same for [COMPANY]?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Example — Accounting for startups:**

```
Hi [NAME],

I help Series A-B startups close their books 10x faster
and be investor-ready at all times.

Last client went from 15-day monthly close to 2-day monthly
close within 60 days.

Worth a quick call to see if that's relevant for [COMPANY]?

[SIGNATURE]
```

**Expected reply rate:** 4-8% (strong with the right audience, feels refreshingly honest)

## Personalization at Scale: The "First Line" Technique

### What It Is

```
The personalized first line is the single most impactful personalization
you can add to a cold email. It's 1 sentence (15-25 words) that shows
you researched this specific person before emailing them.

WHY IT WORKS:
- It's the first thing they read (and see in preview text)
- It immediately separates you from every other cold email
- It signals "this is for you specifically, not a mass blast"
- It creates reciprocity — you invested time, they feel a slight
  obligation to at least read the rest

THE ROI OF FIRST LINES:
- Adding a personalized first line typically increases reply rates by 2-3x
- A mediocre template with a great first line outperforms a great template
  with no personalization
```

### How to Write Custom First Lines Fast (30-60 Seconds Per Prospect)

```
RESEARCH SOURCES (in order of speed):

1. LinkedIn profile (15 seconds)
   - Recent posts or comments they made
   - Job change in the last 6 months
   - Certifications, education, or volunteer work
   - Mutual connections

   First line examples:
   - "Loved your post about [TOPIC] — especially the point about [SPECIFIC DETAIL]."
   - "Congrats on the move to [COMPANY] — [TIMEFRAME] in, how's it going?"
   - "Noticed we're both connected to [MUTUAL] — small world."

2. Company website (15 seconds)
   - Recent blog posts or press releases
   - New product launches or features
   - Team page (new leadership hires)
   - Careers page (what roles they're hiring for)

   First line examples:
   - "Just saw [COMPANY]'s launch of [PRODUCT] — impressive."
   - "Noticed [COMPANY] is hiring for [ROLES] — sounds like a growth phase."
   - "Read your team's blog post on [TOPIC] — great insight on [DETAIL]."

3. Google News (10 seconds)
   - Search "[COMPANY NAME]" in Google News
   - Recent funding, acquisitions, partnerships, awards

   First line examples:
   - "Congrats on [COMPANY]'s Series [X] — exciting milestone."
   - "Saw the news about [COMPANY]'s partnership with [PARTNER]."

4. Their company's social media (10 seconds)
   - Twitter/X, LinkedIn company page
   - Recent announcements or campaigns

5. Industry context (5 seconds)
   - Relevant industry trend or regulation
   - Recent event they likely attended

   First line examples:
   - "After [INDUSTRY EVENT], I imagine [RELEVANT CHALLENGE] is top of mind."
   - "With [INDUSTRY TREND], I'd guess [COMPANY] is evaluating [RELATED TOPIC]."
```

### First Lines to AVOID

```
BAD FIRST LINES (generic, insincere, or cringe):

- "I hope this email finds you well." (← The #1 most overused opening in email history.)
- "I know you're busy, so I'll keep this short." (← Then keep it short without announcing it.)
- "I've been following [COMPANY] for a while and I'm really impressed." (← Vague flattery.)
- "I came across your profile and thought we might have synergies." (← Corporate speak.)
- "I'm [YOUR NAME] from [YOUR COMPANY]." (← Nobody cares who you are yet. Lead with value.)
- "I noticed you're the [TITLE] at [COMPANY]." (← That's not an observation, it's a database query.)
- "My name is [NAME] and I help companies like yours..." (← Self-centered opener.)
```

## CTA Formulas

### The 4 CTA Types

```
1. INTEREST-BASED CTA (lowest friction):
   "Worth exploring?"
   "Is this relevant for [COMPANY]?"
   "Does this resonate?"
   "Interesting, or off base?"

   → BEST FOR: First email, Tier 3 personalization, uncertain fit
   → WHY: Asks for a feeling, not a commitment. Easy to reply "yes" or "no."

2. TIME-BASED CTA (moderate friction):
   "Got 15 minutes this week?"
   "Free for a quick call Tuesday or Thursday?"
   "Worth a 15-minute chat?"
   "Can we hop on a call this week?"

   → BEST FOR: Emails 2-4, warm replies, clear fit
   → WHY: Moves toward a meeting directly. Specific time suggestions increase booking rate.

3. BINARY CTA (forces a response):
   "Yes or no — worth a conversation?"
   "Should I share more, or is this not a fit?"
   "Interested, or should I stop reaching out?"

   → BEST FOR: Emails 5-7, follow-ups, re-engagement
   → WHY: Binary choices are easy to answer. People who weren't going to reply
     often reply "not interested" — which is still useful data.

4. LOW-FRICTION CTA (ask for less than a meeting):
   "Mind if I send over a quick case study?"
   "Can I share a 2-minute video walkthrough?"
   "Want me to send the benchmark report?"

   → BEST FOR: Complex sales, skeptical audiences, value-first approach
   → WHY: Asking for permission to share info is easier than asking for a meeting.
     Once they engage with the content, THEN ask for the meeting.
```

### CTA Rules

```
- ONE CTA per email. Never two. Never "schedule a call or reply with questions."
  Pick one path and commit to it.
- Match CTA friction to relationship stage. Don't ask for a 30-minute demo
  in the first cold email. Build to it.
- Make the CTA a question. Questions get replies. Statements don't.
  "Let me know if you're interested" (statement) vs. "Worth exploring?" (question)
- Avoid vague CTAs. "Let's connect" means nothing. "Got 15 minutes Thursday
  for a call about [SPECIFIC TOPIC]?" is clear.
```

## Formatting Rules

### Layout for Cold Email

```
DO:
- Short paragraphs: 1-2 sentences maximum per paragraph
- Line breaks between every paragraph
- Plain text formatting (no bold, italic, or underline in cold email)
- Use the prospect's first name (not full name, not title)
- Simple signature: Name, title, company, phone (no images, no banners)

DON'T:
- No HTML-rich formatting (it triggers spam filters and looks promotional)
- No images or logos in the email body
- No embedded videos or GIFs
- No bullet points or numbered lists in the first email (looks like a template)
- No attachments (ever — link to resources instead)
- No colored text or backgrounds
- No fancy fonts or font sizes
- No more than 1-2 links (calendar link in CTA is fine; tracking link is fine)
```

### Mobile Optimization

```
60%+ of cold emails are first viewed on mobile.

MOBILE FORMATTING RULES:
- Total email body should be visible without scrolling (under 100 words ideal)
- Subject line hook is in the first 35 characters
- First line of email body is compelling (it's the preview text)
- No tables or complex formatting that breaks on mobile
- Links are easy to tap (full URLs, not tiny hyperlinks)
- Phone number in signature is tap-to-call formatted

TEST: Before sending any campaign, send a test email to yourself and
read it on your phone. If you have to scroll or pinch-zoom, it's too long
or poorly formatted.
```

## Common Copy Mistakes

```
MISTAKE 1: Making it about you
BAD: "We are a leading provider of... We have 10 years of experience... Our team..."
GOOD: "Most [TITLE]s at [SIZE] companies tell us [PROBLEM] is costing them..."
RULE: The ratio of "you/your" to "I/we/our" should be at least 3:1.

MISTAKE 2: Feature dumping
BAD: "Our platform includes AI-powered analytics, real-time dashboards,
      automated reporting, and 50+ integrations..."
GOOD: "We helped [SIMILAR COMPANY] cut reporting time from 10 hours/week to 30 minutes."
RULE: Lead with the outcome, not the feature.

MISTAKE 3: Too much jargon
BAD: "We leverage our proprietary AI-driven synergistic platform to optimize
      cross-functional go-to-market operational efficiency."
GOOD: "We help sales teams book more meetings with less manual work."
RULE: Write at a 6th-grade reading level. If your grandmother wouldn't understand it, simplify.

MISTAKE 4: No personalization
BAD: "Dear Sir/Madam, I wanted to reach out about an exciting opportunity..."
GOOD: "[NAME], saw your talk at [EVENT] — your point about [TOPIC] was spot-on."
RULE: At minimum, include one detail that proves this email was written for them.

MISTAKE 5: Multiple CTAs
BAD: "Would you like to schedule a demo, download our whitepaper, or visit our
      website to learn more?"
GOOD: "Worth a 15-minute call this week?"
RULE: One email, one ask.

MISTAKE 6: Too polite / too many hedges
BAD: "I was just wondering if perhaps, if you have a moment, maybe we could
      possibly chat sometime if it's not too much trouble..."
GOOD: "Worth a quick chat? I'm free Thursday at 2pm or Friday at 10am."
RULE: Be confident and direct. Excessive hedging signals low status.

MISTAKE 7: No clear value proposition
BAD: "I'd love to learn more about your business and see if there's a fit."
GOOD: "We help [ICP] achieve [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]. Worth exploring?"
RULE: Every email must answer "What's in it for me?" in under 5 seconds.
```

## Tone Guide

```
BE:
- Professional but human (like a smart colleague, not a corporation)
- Confident but not arrogant ("we've seen great results" vs. "we're the best")
- Specific but not overwhelming (one data point > three paragraphs)
- Curious about their situation (ask, don't assume)
- Respectful of their time (brevity IS respect)

AVOID:
- Corporate speak ("leverage synergies," "best-in-class," "thought leader")
- Fake urgency ("ACT NOW!" "Only 3 spots left!")
- Passive aggression ("I guess you're too busy to respond...")
- Over-familiarity ("Hey buddy!" to a C-suite executive you've never met)
- Desperation ("I'll do anything to earn your business")
- Manipulation ("I know you're losing money right now because...")
```

## Signature Best Practices

```
COLD EMAIL SIGNATURE (keep it minimal):

[First Name] [Last Name]
[Title], [Company]
[Phone Number]

That's it. No:
- Banner images (trigger spam filters, break on mobile)
- Social media icons (distracting, add links to email)
- Legal disclaimers (makes it look like mass marketing)
- Inspirational quotes
- Long lists of awards or certifications
- Company taglines or slogans
- "Sent from my iPhone" (fake or real, it's unprofessional in cold outreach)
```

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering the email body copy library, verify:

- [ ] At least 5 complete email templates have been written
- [ ] Templates use at least 3 different frameworks (not all PAS or all BAB)
- [ ] Every template is under 125 words (ideally under 100 for first-touch emails)
- [ ] Every template has exactly ONE clear CTA
- [ ] The "you/your" to "I/we/our" ratio is at least 3:1 in each template
- [ ] No spam trigger words are present
- [ ] No HTML formatting, images, or attachments are suggested
- [ ] At least 10 personalized first line examples are provided
- [ ] At least 8 CTA variations are provided across the 4 types
- [ ] Templates are personalized to the user's specific ICP, offer, and industry
- [ ] Tone is professional but human — not corporate, not sycophantic
- [ ] Each template includes a subject line that matches the body
- [ ] Templates would look natural on a mobile screen (short paragraphs, minimal links)
- [ ] Signature recommendation follows the minimal format
- [ ] Templates feel like they came from a real person, not a marketing department
