# ICP Architect

## Purpose

This skill builds a detailed Ideal Customer Profile — a precise, multi-dimensional description of the companies and people most likely to buy. Without a sharp ICP, outreach becomes spray-and-pray. Every message, every targeting decision, every piece of content downstream depends on the clarity of this document.

The ICP is not a guess. It is reverse-engineered from real evidence: the user's best existing clients, their sales data, their market knowledge, and structured analysis. This skill produces a tiered ICP document that directly feeds into lead sourcing, message writing, and campaign targeting.

---

## How This Skill Works

### Step 1: Determine Starting Point

Read the Business Intelligence Brief (output of Business Context Compiler) and classify the user into one of three tracks:

**Track A — Has Great Clients Already**
The user has at least 3-5 clients they consider successful engagements. Use the "Best Client Reverse Engineering" method (Step 2a). This is the strongest foundation.

**Track B — Has Some Clients But Unsure Who Is "Ideal"**
The user has clients but cannot clearly articulate which are best or why. Use the "Client Sorting" exercise (Step 2b) first, then proceed to reverse engineering.

**Track C — Pre-Revenue or New Offer**
The user is launching a new product, entering a new market, or has very few clients. Use the "Hypothesis-Based ICP Builder" (Step 2c) to construct a working ICP based on logic, then flag it for rapid validation.

---

### Step 2a: Best Client Reverse Engineering

This is the highest-signal method. Ask the user to identify their 3-5 best clients, then systematically deconstruct what makes them "best."

#### The Interview

For each best client, ask:

**About the Company:**
1. "What is the company name and what do they do?"
2. "What industry are they in?"
3. "Roughly how many employees do they have?"
4. "What is their approximate annual revenue?"
5. "Where are they located?"
6. "What stage are they at? (Startup, growth, established, enterprise)"
7. "Are they funded? If so, what stage? (Bootstrapped, Seed, Series A, etc.)"
8. "What is their business model? (SaaS, services, marketplace, e-commerce, etc.)"

**About the Relationship:**
9. "How did this client find you? (Referral, inbound, outbound, event, etc.)"
10. "How long did it take from first conversation to signed deal?"
11. "What is the contract value? (Monthly, annual, or project total)"
12. "How long have they been a client?"
13. "Have they expanded their spend with you over time?"
14. "On a scale of 1-10, how easy are they to work with?"
15. "On a scale of 1-10, how profitable is this account?"

**About the Problem:**
16. "What was the specific problem or need that brought them to you?"
17. "What were they doing before you? (Previous solution, doing it manually, nothing)"
18. "What triggered them to start looking for a solution? Was there a specific event or moment?"
19. "What was at stake if they did NOT solve this problem?"

**About the Decision Maker:**
20. "Who was the person you primarily dealt with? (Name and title)"
21. "What is their role in the company? What are they responsible for?"
22. "Were there other people involved in the buying decision? Who?"
23. "What did this person care about most — cost, speed, quality, risk reduction, something else?"
24. "What objections or concerns did they raise during the sales process?"

**About the Result:**
25. "What results have you delivered for this client?"
26. "Would they give you a testimonial or referral?"

#### Pattern Extraction

After collecting data on 3-5 clients, analyze for patterns:

- **Common industries** — Do the best clients cluster in 1-2 industries?
- **Common company size** — Is there a sweet spot for employee count or revenue?
- **Common stage** — Are they mostly growth-stage? Enterprise? Early?
- **Common trigger** — What event or pain drove them to buy?
- **Common decision maker** — Same title? Same level of seniority?
- **Common source** — How did they find you? Is one channel dominant?
- **Common objection** — Did they all worry about the same thing?
- **Common result** — What outcome do they all value most?

These patterns ARE the ICP. The rest of this skill is about structuring and enriching them.

---

### Step 2b: Client Sorting Exercise

If the user has clients but cannot easily identify the "best," use this framework to score them:

| Factor | Weight | Score 1-5 | Weighted Score |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Revenue generated | 25% | | |
| Ease of working together | 20% | | |
| Speed to close | 15% | | |
| Likelihood of renewal / repeat | 15% | | |
| Referral potential | 10% | | |
| Strategic value (case study, logo, learnings) | 10% | | |
| Profitability (revenue minus effort/cost) | 5% | | |

Score every client. Sort by weighted score. Take the top 3-5 and proceed to Step 2a.

### Step 2c: Hypothesis-Based ICP Builder

When there is no client data to reverse-engineer, build a hypothesis ICP:

1. **Start with the problem.** "Who experiences the problem your product solves most acutely? Where is the pain sharpest?"
2. **Work outward.** "What kind of company has that problem? What size? What industry? What stage?"
3. **Identify the buyer.** "Within that company, who owns that problem? Who has the budget and authority to fix it?"
4. **Test with logic.** "Can that person afford your price point? Does their company have the infrastructure to use your solution? Is this a big enough market to sustain your goals?"
5. **Find 5 real companies** that fit the hypothesis. Can the user name them? If they cannot name 5 real companies off the top of their head, the ICP may be too abstract.

Flag this ICP as "Hypothesis — Validate within 30 days" and plan for rapid testing during outreach.

---

### Step 3: Build the Full ICP Profile

Using the data from Step 2 (whichever track), build out each dimension:

#### Demographics

- **Industry/Vertical:** Primary and secondary industries. Be specific — not just "technology" but "B2B SaaS in the HR/People Operations space."
- **Company Size (Employees):** Define a range. Example: "25-200 employees."
- **Company Size (Revenue):** Define a range. Example: "$2M-$20M ARR."
- **Geography:** Countries, regions, cities, or "remote/global."
- **Company Age:** How long have they been operating?
- **Growth Stage:** Startup, Scale-up, Mid-Market, Enterprise, Turnaround.

#### Firmographics

- **Business Model:** SaaS, professional services, e-commerce, marketplace, agency, manufacturing, etc.
- **Technology Stack:** What tools do they use that signal fit? (E.g., "Uses Salesforce" = enterprise sales motion, "Uses HubSpot" = growing team, "Uses spreadsheets" = early stage or underserved)
- **Funding Status:** Bootstrapped, Seed, Series A/B/C, PE-backed, Public.
- **Team Structure:** Do they have the relevant department? (E.g., if you sell to marketing teams, do they have a marketing team?)
- **Revenue Model:** Subscription, project-based, retainer, transactional, advertising.
- **Recent Events:** Hiring spree, new funding round, leadership change, product launch, expansion to new market.

#### Psychographics (Company-Level)

- **Core Pain Points:** The top 3-5 problems your ICP experiences related to what you solve. Write these in the customer's language, not yours.
  - Example: NOT "They need better data integration" but "Their sales team wastes 2 hours a day copy-pasting between tools and they are losing deals because of slow follow-up."
- **Strategic Goals:** What are they trying to achieve this year?
- **Fears and Risks:** What keeps the decision maker up at night?
- **Aspirations:** What does success look like for them?
- **Buying Triggers:** What specific events cause them to start looking for a solution?
  - Examples: Missed quarterly target, new VP hire who wants to make their mark, competitor launched a feature, lost a key client, failed audit, board pressure
- **Common Objections:** What concerns do they raise during the sales process?
  - Examples: "We've tried something like this before and it didn't work," "We don't have the bandwidth to implement," "We need to see ROI within 90 days"

#### Behavioral Signals

- **Where They Spend Time Online:**
  - LinkedIn activity level and content type
  - Industry-specific communities and forums
  - Slack/Discord groups
  - Newsletters they subscribe to
  - Podcasts they listen to
  - Conferences and events they attend
  - Trade publications they read

- **Content They Engage With:**
  - What topics get their attention?
  - Do they prefer long-form or short-form?
  - Case studies or thought leadership?
  - Data-driven or narrative?

- **Buying Behavior:**
  - Do they research heavily or decide quickly?
  - Do they want demos or free trials?
  - Do they check reviews (G2, Capterra)?
  - Are they influenced by peers or by data?
  - Do they buy through inbound or respond to outbound?

---

### Step 4: Decision-Maker Profiling

This is critical for outreach. You are not selling to a company — you are selling to a person.

#### Primary Decision Maker
- **Job Titles:** List 3-5 titles. Be specific. Example: "VP of Sales, Head of Revenue, CRO, Director of Business Development."
- **Seniority Level:** C-Suite, VP, Director, Manager, Individual Contributor.
- **Reporting Structure:** Who do they report to? Who reports to them?
- **Core Responsibilities:** What are they measured on? What do they own?
- **Personal Pain Points:** What makes their job hard? What are they personally frustrated by?
- **Personal Goals:** What does success look like for them individually? (Promotion, hitting quota, getting budget, reducing workload)
- **How They Evaluate Vendors:** Price? Speed? References? ROI proof? Brand reputation?
- **How They Prefer to Be Contacted:** LinkedIn DM? Email? Phone? Introduction?
- **Language They Use:** What jargon, buzzwords, or phrases do they use? What words resonate?

#### Secondary Decision Makers / Influencers
- **Who else is involved?** Technical evaluator, finance/procurement, end user, executive sponsor.
- **What do they care about?** Each influencer has different priorities.
- **How do they influence the decision?** Veto power, recommendation, sign-off on budget.

#### Champions vs. Blockers
- **Champion Profile:** Who inside the target company would be your internal advocate? What motivates them to champion a new solution?
- **Blocker Profile:** Who might resist? IT security? Finance? A competing internal initiative? How do you preempt their concerns?

---

### Step 5: Build the Anti-ICP

Equally important: who is NOT a good fit. This prevents wasted outreach effort.

Ask the user:
1. "Have you ever had a client that was a terrible fit? What made it bad?"
2. "What types of companies waste your time during the sales process but never close?"
3. "Are there industries or company sizes where your product just does not work?"
4. "What red flags do you now recognize early in a sales conversation?"

Document the Anti-ICP:
- **Industries to Avoid:** [List]
- **Company Size Exclusions:** [Too small — cannot afford it. Too big — sales cycle too long. etc.]
- **Red Flag Signals:** [Specific behaviors or characteristics that predict a bad outcome]
- **Common Time-Wasters:** [Profiles that look like fits but never convert]
- **Deal-Breakers:** [Hard criteria that disqualify a prospect immediately]

---

### Step 6: Create ICP Tiers

Not all ICPs are created equal. Segment into tiers to prioritize outreach effort:

#### Tier 1 — Perfect Fit (60% of outreach effort)
These match the ICP on ALL dimensions. Every signal is present. These get personalized, high-effort outreach — custom LinkedIn DMs, researched emails, video messages if applicable.

**Criteria:**
- Matches industry, company size, and revenue range
- Decision maker matches the primary profile
- At least one buying trigger is present
- No anti-ICP red flags
- Has proof point relevance (you have a case study in their space)

#### Tier 2 — Good Fit (30% of outreach effort)
These match on most dimensions but are missing one or two signals. Still worth pursuing with solid outreach, but do not invest the same level of personalization.

**Criteria:**
- Matches industry and company size
- Decision maker is close but not exact (e.g., slightly different title)
- No buying trigger visible yet, but the profile suggests latent need
- No anti-ICP red flags

#### Tier 3 — Opportunistic (10% of outreach effort)
These are long shots — adjacent industries, slightly outside the size range, or new market tests. Use them for volume and learning, not as primary targets.

**Criteria:**
- Matches on some dimensions but not core ones
- Could become a Tier 1 or Tier 2 in the future
- Useful for testing new market hypotheses

## Quality Checklist

Before marking this skill complete, verify:

- [ ] **Industry is specific** — Not "technology" but "B2B SaaS in the fintech space" or "E-commerce brands doing $1-10M revenue."
- [ ] **Company size has clear boundaries** — Both a floor and a ceiling for employee count and/or revenue.
- [ ] **Decision maker is a real title** — Not "business owners" but "VP of Marketing" or "CEO of companies with 10-50 employees."
- [ ] **Pain points are in customer language** — Written the way the customer would describe it, not how the seller describes it.
- [ ] **At least 3 buying triggers are identified** — Specific events that create urgency.
- [ ] **Anti-ICP is defined** — Clear criteria for who to avoid.
- [ ] **Tiers are differentiated** — Tier 1, 2, and 3 have meaningfully different criteria.
- [ ] **Real company examples are listed** — At least 3-5 named companies per tier (or flagged for the user to provide).
- [ ] **The user has confirmed the ICP** — They agree this accurately describes their best customers.
- [ ] **Confidence level is noted** — Clearly marked as data-backed or hypothesis.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **ICP is too broad.** "Any B2B company with 10-10,000 employees" is not an ICP. If it describes more than a few hundred thousand companies, narrow it.

2. **ICP is too narrow.** "Series B SaaS companies in Portland with exactly 47 employees" leaves no room for outreach at scale. There should be at least a few thousand companies that fit Tier 1.

3. **Confusing the end user with the buyer.** The person who uses your product may not be the person who decides to buy it. Profile the decision maker, not just the user.

4. **Listing pain points YOU think they have instead of pains THEY feel.** "They need better data governance" is your framing. "They spend 3 days every month manually pulling reports for the board and they are terrified of getting a number wrong" is their reality.

5. **Ignoring the Anti-ICP.** Every hour spent on a bad-fit prospect is an hour not spent on a good-fit one. The Anti-ICP is a force multiplier.

6. **Treating ICP as static.** The ICP is a hypothesis until validated by outreach data. Plan to revisit it after 30 days of campaigns.

## Handoff

When this skill is complete, the ICP Document is saved and made available to:
- **Offer Positioning Engine** — Uses pain points, goals, and decision-maker profile to craft positioning that resonates.
- **Competitive Landscape Mapper** — Uses industry and decision-maker context to identify relevant competitors.
- **Sales Asset Auditor** — Uses ICP to evaluate whether existing assets speak to the right audience.
- **All outreach skills** — The ICP directly determines who gets messaged, what the message says, and how it is personalized.
