# Competitive Landscape Mapper

## Purpose

This skill maps the competitive landscape so that outreach is never done in a vacuum. Every prospect the Sales OS contacts is already aware of alternatives — competitors they have heard of, solutions they have tried, or the option to do nothing. This skill ensures that every message, every follow-up, and every sales conversation is informed by what the competition is doing and where the gaps are.

The output is not a traditional competitive analysis deck. It is an outreach-focused competitive intelligence brief — designed to arm the user with specific talk tracks, positioning angles, and differentiation hooks that can be deployed in LinkedIn DMs, cold emails, and sales calls.

---

## How This Skill Works

### Step 1: Identify the Competitive Universe

There are five categories of alternatives a prospect considers. Most users only think about category 1. All five matter for outreach.

#### Category 1: Direct Competitors
Companies that sell a nearly identical product or service to the same ICP.

**How to identify them:**
- Ask the user: "Who do you most often compete against in deals? When a prospect says 'we are also looking at...', who do they name?"
- Ask: "If you were your ideal customer and searched Google for a solution to your problem, what companies would you find?"
- Ask: "Who are the 3-5 companies you consider your main competitors?"
- Check the Business Intelligence Brief for any competitors already mentioned.

#### Category 2: Indirect Competitors
Companies that solve the same underlying problem but with a fundamentally different approach.

**How to identify them:**
- Ask: "Is there a completely different type of solution that addresses the same problem? For example, if you sell marketing automation software, an indirect competitor might be a marketing agency."
- Ask: "What was your client doing before they found you? If the answer is not a competing product, it might be an indirect competitor."

#### Category 3: DIY / Internal Solutions
The prospect builds something internally instead of buying.

**How to identify them:**
- Ask: "Do some prospects decide to build this capability in-house instead of buying? How often does this happen?"
- Ask: "What does the internal version usually look like? (Spreadsheets, manual processes, a junior hire, etc.)"

#### Category 4: Hire-to-Solve
The prospect hires a person or team instead of buying a product/service.

**How to identify them:**
- Ask: "Do prospects ever decide to hire someone to handle this instead of working with you? What role would that be?"
- Ask: "What does that hire typically cost them? How long until that person is productive?"

#### Category 5: Do Nothing / Status Quo
The prospect decides the problem is not urgent enough to solve right now.

**How to identify them:**
- Ask: "How often do deals stall because the prospect decides to wait? What reasons do they give?"
- Ask: "What is the actual cost of doing nothing? Do your prospects understand that cost, or do they underestimate it?"

### Step 2: Build Competitor Profiles

For each direct and indirect competitor identified (focus on the top 3-5), build a profile using publicly available information.

#### Information Sources (No Paid Tools Required)

1. **Their website** — Homepage messaging, pricing page, about page, blog content, case studies.
2. **Their LinkedIn company page** — Follower count, employee count, recent posts, hiring activity.
3. **Key people's LinkedIn profiles** — Founders, sales leaders, content they post, language they use.
4. **Review sites** — G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, Google Reviews. Read both positive and negative reviews.
5. **Their content** — Blog posts, podcasts, webinars, YouTube channel. What topics do they cover? What positioning do they use?
6. **Job postings** — What roles are they hiring for? This reveals strategy. Hiring SDRs = investing in outbound. Hiring engineers = building product. Hiring in a new city = expanding to new markets.
7. **Social media activity** — How active are they? What content gets engagement? What is their tone?
8. **Crunchbase or similar** — Funding history, investors, team size trajectory.
9. **The user's direct experience** — "Have you ever received a cold email or LinkedIn DM from this competitor? What did it say?"
10. **Client feedback** — "Have any of your clients mentioned they previously used or evaluated this competitor? What did they say about the experience?"

### Step 3: Competitive Positioning Matrix

Map all competitors (including yourself) on the dimensions that matter most to the ICP.

#### How to Choose Dimensions

Ask the user:
- "When your ideal customer is evaluating solutions, what are the 3-4 factors they care about most? Price? Speed of results? Ease of use? Depth of service? Industry expertise? Something else?"

Common dimension pairs:
- Price vs. Quality/Depth of Service
- Ease of Use vs. Feature Richness
- Speed of Results vs. Comprehensiveness
- Self-Serve vs. Done-For-You
- Generalist vs. Specialist
- Established/Safe vs. Innovative/Cutting-Edge

#### Build the Matrix

Create a 2x2 matrix using the top two dimensions. Place each competitor (and yourself) on the grid.

```
                    [Dimension 1: High]
                          |
         Quadrant 2       |       Quadrant 1
         [Competitor B]   |       [YOU]
                          |       [Competitor A]
 [Dimension 2: Low] ------+------ [Dimension 2: High]
                          |
         Quadrant 3       |       Quadrant 4
         [Competitor D]   |       [Competitor C]
                          |
                    [Dimension 1: Low]
```

**Analysis Questions:**
- Where is the crowded quadrant? (Where are most competitors clustered?)
- Where is the empty quadrant? (Is there an underserved position?)
- Where are you? Is your position defensible? Is it where the ICP wants to buy?
- If you are in the crowded quadrant, how do you break out? If you are in an empty quadrant, is it empty because nobody wants to be there, or because nobody has claimed it yet?

### Step 4: Gap Analysis

Identify specific gaps in the competitive landscape that represent opportunities for outreach messaging.

#### The Gap Framework

For each competitor, analyze:

**Positioning Gaps — What They Do Not Say**
- What problems does the ICP have that this competitor does not address in their messaging?
- What industries or segments does this competitor ignore?
- What pain points are they silent on?

**Capability Gaps — What They Do Not Do**
- What features or services does the ICP want that this competitor does not offer?
- Where do their clients express frustration (check negative reviews)?
- What part of the customer journey do they leave unsupported?

**Proof Gaps — What They Cannot Prove**
- Do they make claims without evidence?
- Are their case studies outdated or from irrelevant industries?
- Do they lack social proof in the specific vertical you are targeting?

**Experience Gaps — Where They Fall Short**
- What is the onboarding experience like? (Common complaint area)
- How responsive is their support?
- What happens after the sale? (Many competitors are strong at sales but weak at delivery)

**Compile into Outreach Opportunities:**

For each gap, write an outreach angle that exploits it:

| Gap | Competitor | Outreach Angle |
|-----|-----------|----------------|
| [No case studies in fintech] | [Competitor A] | "Unlike generic solutions, we specialize in fintech — here is a case study from [Similar Company]." |
| [Long onboarding time] | [Competitor B] | "Most solutions take 3-6 months to show results. We guarantee first results in 30 days." |
| [No support after sale] | [Competitor C] | "We do not just set it up and disappear — every client gets a dedicated strategist for the full engagement." |

### Step 5: "Steal the Playbook" — Competitor Outreach Analysis

Study how competitors are reaching the same prospects you want to reach.

#### What to Investigate

**LinkedIn Content Analysis:**
- What topics do they post about?
- What format gets them the most engagement? (Text posts, carousels, videos, polls)
- How often do they post?
- What language and framing do they use?
- What can you learn from their approach? What can you do better?

**Their Cold Outreach (If You Have Received It):**
- Ask the user: "Have you ever received a cold email or LinkedIn message from a competitor? If so, can you share it?"
- Analyze: What hook did they use? What was the call to action? Was it personalized? What was strong about it? What was weak?

**Their Sales Pages and Funnels:**
- What is the flow from first touch to booking a call?
- What lead magnets are they offering?
- What language is on their landing pages?
- What objections are they pre-empting?

**Their Client Acquisition Channels:**
- Are they investing in content marketing? (Blog, podcast, YouTube)
- Are they running paid ads? (Check Facebook Ad Library, LinkedIn Ads)
- Are they active in communities? (Slack groups, Reddit, industry forums)
- Are they speaking at events or hosting webinars?
- Are they running referral or partner programs?

#### What to Steal (Ethically)

- **Messaging frameworks that work.** If a competitor's LinkedIn post about a specific pain point gets 500 likes, that pain point resonates. Use it in your outreach (with your own angle).
- **Content topics with proven demand.** If they are writing about a topic and getting engagement, the ICP cares about it.
- **Proof structures.** How do they present case studies? What format works? Adapt it.
- **Channel strategies.** If they are investing heavily in LinkedIn outreach and growing fast, that channel works for your ICP.

#### What NOT to Steal
- Exact copy or templates (plagiarism destroys credibility)
- Proprietary data or client information
- Claims you cannot back up yourself

### Step 6: Competitive Talk Tracks

Build specific scripts for handling competitive situations in outreach and sales conversations.

#### Talk Track 1: "We Are Already Using [Competitor]"

**Framework: Acknowledge → Validate → Differentiate → Offer Value**

Template:
"That makes sense — [Competitor] is solid at [what they do well]. A lot of our clients actually came to us from [Competitor] because [specific gap or limitation]. The main difference is [your key advantage]. I am not suggesting you switch — but would it be useful to see how [specific result you deliver differently]? No pressure either way."

**Customize for each competitor:**
- [Competitor A]: "They are great at [X], but clients tell us they struggle with [Y]. We solve that by..."
- [Competitor B]: "Solid platform, but most teams find [specific limitation]. Our approach is..."
- [Competitor C]: "Good for [segment], but if you are [ICP-specific characteristic], you might find..."

#### Talk Track 2: "We Are Evaluating Several Options"

**Framework: Welcome Comparison → Set the Criteria → Plant Your Advantage**

Template:
"That is smart — you should compare options. When evaluating, the three things I would pay attention to are [Criterion 1], [Criterion 2], and [Criterion 3]. Those tend to be the factors that determine whether a solution actually works in practice vs. just looks good in a demo. Happy to walk you through how we stack up on those specifically."

Note: Choose criteria where you are strongest. You are framing the evaluation in your favor without disparaging anyone.

#### Talk Track 3: "We Tried Something Like This Before and It Did Not Work"

**Framework: Empathize → Diagnose → Differentiate Your Approach**

Template:
"I hear that a lot, actually. Can I ask — what specifically did not work? [Listen]. That makes sense. The reason that happens with most [type of solution] is [root cause]. Our approach is different because [specific mechanism]. For example, [proof point of a client who had the same experience and succeeded with you]."

#### Talk Track 4: "We Handle This Internally"

**Framework: Respect → Quantify the Hidden Cost → Offer a Comparison Point**

Template:
"Totally understand — it is great that you have the capability in-house. Curious question: roughly how many hours per week does your team spend on [the task]? [Listen]. That is pretty common. When we did the math with [similar company], they found that the internal approach was costing them [amount in salary, time, or opportunity cost] per month — and they were getting [result metric]. With our approach, they got [better result] for [lower cost or effort]. Worth a 15-minute comparison?"

#### Talk Track 5: "Now Is Not the Right Time"

**Framework: Agree → Introduce Cost of Delay → Offer Low-Commitment Next Step**

Template:
"That is fair — timing matters. Quick question before I let you go: what would need to be true for this to be the right time? [Listen]. Got it. The reason I ask is that most of our clients tell us they wish they had started [X months] sooner because [cost of delay — lost revenue, missed opportunities, compounding problem]. I am not trying to create false urgency — just want to share that [brief proof point]. Would it make sense to [low-commitment action — save a slot, set a reminder, share a resource] so this is easy to pick back up when the timing is right?"

## Quality Checklist

Before marking this skill complete, verify:

- [ ] **At least 3 direct competitors are profiled** — With real data, not assumptions.
- [ ] **Indirect competitors and non-purchase alternatives are documented** — DIY, hire, and do-nothing are addressed.
- [ ] **Positioning matrix is built** — On dimensions the ICP actually cares about.
- [ ] **Gap analysis reveals at least 3 exploitable gaps** — Specific, not generic ("they are worse than us").
- [ ] **Talk tracks are written for the top 5 competitive scenarios** — Customized per competitor where possible.
- [ ] **Differentiation angles are outreach-ready** — Each has a proof point and example copy.
- [ ] **Competitor outreach tactics are analyzed** — You know what the ICP is already receiving from competitors.
- [ ] **Honest assessment of competitor strengths** — No blindspots about where competitors genuinely outperform.
- [ ] **The user has confirmed the analysis** — They agree with the competitive positioning.
- [ ] **All claims are defensible** — Nothing in the talk tracks or angles is dishonest or unverifiable.

---

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Only analyzing direct competitors.** The biggest competitor is usually "do nothing." Address it.

2. **Being dismissive of competitors.** "They are terrible" is not a strategy. Prospects respect vendors who acknowledge competitors' strengths while clearly articulating a different value.

3. **Negative selling.** Never trash a competitor in outreach. Instead, plant criteria that favor you: "The three things I would look for are..." and choose criteria where you win.

4. **Outdated intelligence.** Competitors change fast. Flag the date of analysis and plan to refresh quarterly.

5. **Analysis paralysis.** The goal is not a 50-page research report. It is outreach-ready competitive angles. Keep it practical.

6. **Ignoring what competitors do well.** If a competitor has a genuinely better feature or lower price, pretending otherwise backfires when the prospect discovers the truth. Instead, frame why your tradeoff is the right one for the ICP.

- **Campaign skills** — Competitor client lists can become targeted outreach campaigns.
