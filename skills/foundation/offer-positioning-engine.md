# Offer Positioning Engine

## Purpose

This skill takes the raw material from the Business Intelligence Brief and ICP Document and crystallizes it into razor-sharp positioning. The output is not marketing fluff — it is the precise language and framing that will be used in every LinkedIn DM, cold email, follow-up sequence, and sales conversation that the Sales OS generates.

Great positioning means the prospect reads your message and thinks: "This is exactly what I need right now." Bad positioning means they think: "Another vendor pitching me something generic." The difference is in the specificity, the framing, and the proof — all of which this skill produces.

---

## How This Skill Works

### Step 1: Extract Raw Inputs

Pull the following from the Business Intelligence Brief and ICP Document:

- **What you sell** — Product/service description, pricing, delivery model
- **Who you sell to** — ICP demographics, decision-maker profile
- **What problem you solve** — Pain points in customer language
- **What results you deliver** — Proof points, case studies, metrics
- **How you are different** — Current differentiators (even if vague)
- **Brand voice** — Tone, communication style, words to use and avoid

---

### Step 2: The Core Positioning Framework

Build positioning across four pillars:

#### Pillar 1: Transformation (Before → After)

This is the most powerful positioning element. People do not buy products — they buy the transformation from a painful current state to a desired future state.

**Exercise: The Before/After Grid**

| Dimension | Before (Without You) | After (With You) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Daily experience** | [What does the workday feel like?] | [What does it feel like now?] |
| **Status** | [How are they perceived?] | [How are they perceived now?] |
| **Capability** | [What can they NOT do?] | [What can they now do?] |
| **Emotional state** | [Frustrated? Anxious? Overwhelmed?] | [Confident? Relieved? In control?] |
| **Measurable outcome** | [Specific metric — before] | [Specific metric — after] |
| **Time/effort** | [Hours wasted on this problem] | [Hours reclaimed] |
| **Risk** | [What could go wrong?] | [What risk is eliminated?] |

**Questions to ask if the grid is thin:**
- "Walk me through a typical day for your client before they start working with you. What is frustrating them?"
- "Now walk me through their day 90 days after working with you. What has changed?"
- "If I talked to your best client right now and asked 'What is the biggest difference since you started working with [Company]?', what would they say?"
- "What was the specific moment when a client realized this was working? What happened?"

**Write the Transformation Statement:**

Format: "[ICP] who struggle with [Before State] use [Your Solution] to [After State] — typically seeing [Specific Measurable Result] within [Timeframe]."

Example: "B2B SaaS companies who struggle with unpredictable pipeline use our outbound system to generate 15-30 qualified meetings per month — typically seeing results within the first 3 weeks."

---

#### Pillar 2: Mechanism (How It Works)

Prospects need to understand HOW you deliver the transformation. Without a mechanism, the promise feels like hype.

**The Mechanism Framework:**

1. **Name it.** Give your process or methodology a name. This creates perceived intellectual property and makes it feel proprietary. Examples: "The Revenue Engine Framework," "The 90-Day Growth Sprint," "The Precision Outbound Method."

2. **Break it into steps.** 3-5 clear steps that take the client from their current state to the promised result. Each step should feel logical and inevitable.

3. **Explain why it works.** What is the underlying principle or insight that makes this approach effective? This is the "aha" moment.

4. **Differentiate the mechanism.** Why does YOUR approach work better than the obvious alternative? What do others get wrong that you get right?

**Questions to ask:**
- "Walk me through what happens after a client signs. What is step one? Step two? How do you deliver results?"
- "What is the core insight or principle that your approach is built on? What do you understand that most people in your space do not?"
- "If you had to explain your process on a napkin in 3 steps, what would they be?"
- "Do you have a name for your process or methodology? If not, what would capture its essence?"

**Write the Mechanism Statement:**

Format: "We use [Named Method] — a [Number]-step process that [Brief Description]. Unlike [Common Alternative], we [Key Differentiator], which is why [Proof of Effectiveness]."

Example: "We use the Precision Outbound Method — a 4-step process that identifies your highest-probability buyers and reaches them with hyper-relevant messaging across LinkedIn and email. Unlike mass-blast approaches, we research each prospect individually, which is why our clients see 3-5x higher response rates than industry average."

---

#### Pillar 3: Proof (Why They Should Believe You)

Claims without proof are just opinions. Layer proof throughout your positioning.

**The Proof Hierarchy (strongest to weakest):**

1. **Specific client results with names and numbers.** "We helped [Company X] increase their pipeline by 340% in 90 days."
2. **Aggregate results.** "Our clients average a 12x ROI within the first 6 months."
3. **Named client testimonials.** Direct quotes from real people with titles and companies.
4. **Anonymous case studies.** "A Series B fintech company came to us with..."
5. **Process proof.** Screenshots, walkthrough videos, or behind-the-scenes looks at your work.
6. **Credentials and certifications.** Industry certifications, awards, media features.
7. **Social proof signals.** Number of clients served, years in business, team expertise.
8. **Logical proof.** If you do not have data yet: "Here is why this works, based on [principle/framework]."

**Questions to ask:**
- "What is the single most impressive result you have delivered for a client? Give me the specific numbers."
- "Do you have 2-3 other results you can point to?"
- "Do you have any client quotes I can use? Even informal ones — something a client said in an email or on a call?"
- "Have you been featured in any publications, podcasts, or industry events?"
- "How many clients have you served in total? How many are you working with right now?"
- "What is the average ROI or outcome your clients experience?"

**Build the Proof Arsenal:**

Create a prioritized list of every proof point available, ranked by the hierarchy above. For each:
- The claim it supports
- The specific language to use when referencing it
- Where it can be deployed (LinkedIn DM, email, follow-up, landing page)

---

#### Pillar 4: Differentiator (Why You vs. Alternatives)

The prospect has options. You need to make the contrast sharp and specific.

**Five Types of Alternatives to Position Against:**

1. **Direct competitors** — Other companies selling a similar product/service.
2. **Indirect competitors** — Different solutions to the same problem.
3. **Do it themselves** — The prospect builds an internal solution.
4. **Hire someone** — The prospect hires a full-time employee instead.
5. **Do nothing** — The prospect decides the problem is not urgent enough to solve.

**For each alternative, define:**
- What the prospect gains by choosing that alternative
- What the prospect loses or risks by choosing that alternative
- Your specific advantage over that alternative

**The Differentiation Test:**

For each claimed differentiator, ask:
1. "Is this actually true? Can we back it up?"
2. "Is this unique? Could a competitor say the same thing?"
3. "Does the ICP care about this? Is it relevant to their decision?"
4. "Is this specific enough? Or is it a vague claim anyone could make?"

A differentiator must pass ALL four questions. "We provide great service" fails #2, #3, and #4. "We guarantee a 15-minute response time during business hours, tracked in a shared dashboard" passes all four.

**Questions to ask:**
- "When a prospect is choosing between you and an alternative, what is the deciding factor?"
- "What have clients told you was the reason they chose you?"
- "What can you do that your competitors genuinely cannot? Not 'do better' — actually cannot do."
- "What would a prospect lose if they went with a competitor instead of you?"
- "Why should someone solve this problem now rather than wait 6 months?"

---

### Step 3: The One-Liner Test

Distill everything into one sentence. This is the hardest part and the most important.

**The Formula:**

"I/We help [Specific ICP] [Achieve Specific Outcome] through [Mechanism/Approach] — without [Pain They Want to Avoid]."

**Iteration Process:**

1. Write a first draft.
2. Apply the "So What?" test — read it back and ask "So what?" If the answer is not obvious, rewrite.
3. Apply the "Who Cares?" test — show it to someone outside the industry. Do they understand what you do and why it matters?
4. Apply the "Prove It" test — is there a proof point you can immediately attach to this claim?
5. Apply the "Say It Out Loud" test — read it aloud. Does it sound natural? Would you actually say this in a conversation?

**Examples of strong one-liners:**

- "We help B2B SaaS companies book 20+ qualified demos per month through done-for-you LinkedIn and email outreach — without hiring a sales team."
- "We help e-commerce brands cut customer acquisition costs by 40% through AI-powered ad creative testing — with results in the first 14 days."
- "We help professional services firms turn their expertise into a predictable client acquisition system — without spending a dollar on ads."

**Red flags in one-liners:**
- Contains jargon the ICP would not use
- Makes a claim with no implied proof
- Could describe 100 different companies
- Takes more than 10 seconds to say aloud
- Does not mention the ICP at all

---

### Step 4: Build the Full Messaging Stack

Using the four pillars and the one-liner, build a complete messaging toolkit:

#### The Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)

Structure:
1. **Hook** — State the problem in a way that creates recognition. (2 seconds)
2. **Bridge** — Connect the problem to your solution. (3 seconds)
3. **Solution** — What you do and for whom. (10 seconds)
4. **Proof** — One specific result or credential. (5 seconds)
5. **Invitation** — What happens next. (5 seconds)

Template:
"Most [ICP] struggle with [Problem]. The usual approach is [Common but flawed alternative], which leads to [Negative outcome]. We take a different approach — [Mechanism summary]. For example, [Proof point]. If that sounds relevant, I would love to [Next step]."

#### The Value Proposition Statement (3-5 sentences)

Template:
"[Company] helps [ICP] achieve [Primary Outcome]. Using our [Named Method], we [How it works in one sentence]. Our clients typically see [Measurable Result] within [Timeframe]. Unlike [Alternative], we [Key Differentiator]. We have helped [Social proof number] companies including [1-2 named logos if available]."

#### Key Differentiators (Top 3)

For each:
- **The Differentiator:** [One sentence]
- **Why It Matters to the ICP:** [One sentence]
- **Proof That It Is Real:** [One sentence]

#### Proof Points (Top 5)

For each:
- **The Claim:** [What you are asserting]
- **The Evidence:** [Specific data, quote, or example]
- **Best Context to Use It:** [LinkedIn DM, email, follow-up, call]

#### Objection Pre-Empts (Top 5)

For each common objection the ICP is likely to raise:
- **The Objection:** [What they will say or think]
- **The Root Concern:** [What is really behind the objection]
- **The Pre-Empt:** [How to address it before they raise it — in your positioning, not as a rebuttal]
- **The Direct Response:** [If they do raise it, how to respond]

---

### Step 5: The "So What?" Stress Test

Run every piece of positioning through this gauntlet:

1. Read the statement aloud.
2. Ask: "So what? Why does this matter to the ICP?"
3. If the answer is not immediately obvious, the statement is too abstract. Make it more specific.
4. Repeat until the statement lands in 3 seconds or less.

**Example:**

- Draft: "We use AI to optimize outreach." → So what?
- Revised: "We use AI to write outreach that gets 3x more responses." → So what?
- Revised: "We use AI to write outreach that gets 3x more responses, which means you book more meetings without sending more messages." → This lands.

---

### Step 6: Offer Stack Structuring

If the user has multiple ways to engage, structure them as a stack:

#### Entry Offer (Low Friction)
- **What:** Free or low-cost way to experience value. (Audit, assessment, free trial, workshop, template)
- **Purpose:** Gets the foot in the door. Proves capability with zero risk.
- **Price:** Free or sub-$500.
- **Best For:** Prospects who are interested but not ready to commit.

#### Core Offer (Primary Revenue)
- **What:** The main product or service being sold.
- **Purpose:** Delivers the primary transformation.
- **Price:** [Main price point]
- **Best For:** Prospects with confirmed need and budget.

#### Premium Offer (Maximum Value)
- **What:** The most comprehensive engagement — includes everything.
- **Purpose:** Captures maximum value from the best clients.
- **Price:** 2-5x the core offer.
- **Best For:** Tier 1 ICP companies with high urgency.

For each offer:
- One-line description
- Who it is for
- What is included
- What result it delivers
- How it leads to the next tier

---

### Step 7: Message-Market Fit Testing

Before this positioning is deployed in outreach, validate it:

**Quick Validation Methods:**
1. **The Friend Test** — Explain your positioning to someone unfamiliar with your business. Can they repeat it back accurately?
2. **The Competitor Scan** — Does any competitor use nearly identical language? If yes, sharpen your differentiators.
3. **The LinkedIn Test** — Post your one-liner as a LinkedIn status update. Does it get engagement from your ICP?
4. **The Cold DM Test** — Send the value proposition to 10 ICP prospects. Track response rates.
5. **The Existing Client Test** — Share the positioning with a current client. Ask: "Is this what you would tell a friend about working with us?"

Flag any positioning as "untested" until it has been validated by at least one method above.

## Quality Checklist

Before marking this skill complete, verify:

- [ ] **One-liner passes the "Say It Out Loud" test** — It sounds natural, not corporate.
- [ ] **Transformation is specific** — Before/After states include measurable differences, not vague improvements.
- [ ] **Mechanism has a name** — The process feels proprietary and structured, not generic.
- [ ] **At least 3 proof points are documented** — Even if informal, there is evidence behind the claims.
- [ ] **Differentiators pass the 4-question test** — True, unique, relevant, and specific.
- [ ] **Objection pre-empts are ready** — The top 5 objections have prepared responses.
- [ ] **Offer stack is defined** — Entry, core, and premium tiers are clear.
- [ ] **Every statement has been stress-tested with "So What?"** — No abstract claims remain.
- [ ] **The user has confirmed the positioning** — They would use this language in a real conversation.
- [ ] **Validation plan is set** — At least one testing method is planned.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Positioning that sounds like everyone else.** If you can swap in a competitor's name and the statement still works, it is not differentiated.

2. **Leading with features instead of outcomes.** "We have an AI-powered platform with 47 integrations" vs. "We help you book 30% more meetings by automating the repetitive parts of outreach." The second one sells.

3. **Proof-free claims.** "We are the best in the industry" means nothing without evidence. Even "We have helped 12 companies in your space" is infinitely more credible.

4. **Overcomplicating the mechanism.** If the process has 12 steps, the prospect zones out. Simplify to 3-5 steps. Details come later in the sales conversation.

5. **Ignoring "do nothing" as a competitor.** Most deals are not lost to competitors — they are lost to inaction. Your positioning must create urgency around the cost of delay.

6. **Making the company the hero instead of the customer.** The customer is the hero. You are the guide. Frame everything around their transformation, not your capabilities.
