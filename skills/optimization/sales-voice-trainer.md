# Sales Voice Trainer

## Purpose

Extract and codify your unique writing voice so that every piece of outreach — emails, LinkedIn DMs, follow-ups, connection notes — sounds authentically like you. AI-generated copy that doesn't match how you actually communicate creates a jarring disconnect: a prospect reads a polished, formal email and then hops on a call with someone casual and direct. That mismatch kills trust before the conversation even starts. This skill builds a Voice Profile that ensures consistency across every touchpoint.

---

## Why Voice Matters in Sales Outreach

### The Trust Gap Problem

When a prospect receives your cold email and later gets on a call with you, they unconsciously compare the two experiences. If the email sounds like a corporate marketing team and you sound like a laid-back founder, they feel misled — even if they can't articulate why.

This creates a "trust gap" that manifests as:
- Prospects seeming guarded or skeptical on calls despite positive email replies
- Conversations that feel like you're starting from zero even though they engaged with your outreach
- Lower close rates despite healthy meeting volume
- Prospects saying things like "you're different than I expected"

### The Authenticity Advantage

When your outreach sounds exactly like you, the opposite happens:
- Prospects feel like they already know you when they get on the call
- Conversations flow naturally because expectations are aligned
- Trust builds faster because there's no dissonance
- Your outreach stands out because real voices are rare in a sea of generic AI copy
- Replies are more conversational (they match the tone you set)

### The Voice Consistency Principle

**You are one person across all channels.** Your LinkedIn DMs should sound like you. Your emails should sound like you. Your follow-ups should sound like you. When a prospect encounters you on LinkedIn, gets an email, and then joins a call — it should feel like talking to the same human throughout.

## The Voice Extraction Process

### Step 1: Collect Writing Samples (10-15 samples minimum, 20+ ideal)

Gather examples of your natural, unfiltered writing. The key word is NATURAL — not carefully edited blog posts or formal business writing, but how you actually communicate when you're not overthinking it.

**Best sources (ranked by authenticity):**

1. **Text messages / WhatsApp messages** — Your most natural voice. How you communicate with friends and close colleagues.
2. **Slack / Teams messages** — How you write in a work context but informally.
3. **LinkedIn DMs you've sent** — Your semi-professional conversational voice.
4. **Emails to people you know well** — Internal emails, emails to friends/partners.
5. **LinkedIn posts or comments** — Your "public professional" voice.
6. **Cold emails you've written yourself** — If you've done outreach before, these show your sales voice.
7. **Voice message transcripts** — Transcribe a few voice notes you've sent. This captures your spoken rhythm in text form.

**What NOT to use:**
- Formal business documents (contracts, proposals, reports)
- Content written by someone else on your behalf
- Copy that was heavily edited by a team
- AI-generated content you've used before (circular reference)
- Academic or technical writing (unless that IS your natural voice)

**How to collect:**
- Screenshot or copy-paste 10-20 messages/posts
- Include a mix: some short (1-2 sentences), some longer (paragraph+)
- Include different contexts: professional, semi-casual, excited about something, explaining something, making an ask

### Step 2: Analyze Patterns Across Dimensions

Review all samples and look for consistent patterns across these dimensions:

#### Sentence Length
- Does the user write in short, punchy sentences? ("Love this. Let's do it. Tuesday works.")
- Or flowing, detailed sentences with multiple clauses?
- Mix of both? (Short for emphasis, longer for explanation?)
- Average sentence word count across samples

**Scoring:**
- 1-8 words average: Very punchy/direct
- 8-15 words average: Balanced
- 15+ words average: Detailed/flowing

#### Vocabulary Level
- Simple, everyday words ("help," "grow," "fix," "build")
- Industry jargon and technical terms ("scalable GTM motion," "unit economics," "churn reduction")
- Sophisticated but accessible ("streamline," "accelerate," "catalyst")
- Colloquial/slang ("crushed it," "game-changer," "legit")

**Note which specific words appear repeatedly.** These are signature vocabulary.

#### Tone
Rate on these spectrums:
- Casual ←————→ Formal
- Friendly ←————→ Authoritative
- Warm ←————→ Direct
- Humble ←————→ Confident
- Playful ←————→ Serious
- Empathetic ←————→ Results-focused

Most people are NOT at the extremes. Capture the specific position: "Casual-professional. Friendly but direct. Confident without being arrogant."

#### Punctuation Habits
- Em dashes (—) usage: frequent, occasional, never?
- Ellipses (...) usage: trailing thoughts, creating suspense, never?
- Exclamation points: frequent (enthusiastic!), occasional (for emphasis!), never (too corporate)?
- Question marks: lots of questions, or mostly statements?
- Parenthetical asides (like this): common or rare?
- ALL CAPS for emphasis: yes or no?
- Emoji usage: frequent, occasional, never? Which specific emojis?

#### Opening Patterns
How do they typically start messages?
- Jump right in: "So I was thinking..."
- Greeting first: "Hey [Name],"
- Question lead: "Quick question —"
- Observation lead: "Noticed something interesting..."
- Direct statement: "I have an idea."

#### Closing Patterns
How do they sign off?
- Abrupt: just ends with the last thought
- Question: ends with a question
- Casual: "Cheers," "Talk soon," "LMK"
- Professional: "Best," "Thanks," "Looking forward to hearing from you"
- Warm: "Hope that helps," "Rooting for you"

#### Humor Usage
- None (all business)
- Dry/witty (understated observations)
- Self-deprecating ("I'm probably overthinking this, but...")
- Enthusiastic ("This is going to be awesome")
- Sarcastic (use carefully in outreach)

#### Directness Level
- Gets to the point immediately (no build-up)
- Provides context before the ask (builds the case)
- Asks permission before diving in ("Mind if I share a thought?")
- Indirect (hints at the point, lets the reader connect the dots)

#### Formality Spectrum
- Uses contractions: "I'm," "don't," "we're" vs. "I am," "do not," "we are"
- Sentence fragments: "Big fan of this." vs. always complete sentences
- Slang/colloquial: "gonna," "kinda," "tbh" vs. standard English
- Addressing people: "Hey," "Hi," "Dear," first name, full name, title

## Applying the Voice Profile to Sales OS Outputs

### Subject Lines

The Voice Profile should influence subject line style:

**If voice is casual and direct:**
- "quick thought about [COMPANY]"
- "[NAME] — idea for you"
- "this might help"

**If voice is professional and polished:**
- "[COMPANY] + [YOUR SERVICE] — worth exploring?"
- "Idea for [COMPANY]'s [INITIATIVE]"
- "Regarding [SPECIFIC TOPIC]"

**If voice is curious and question-oriented:**
- "how does [COMPANY] handle [CHALLENGE]?"
- "question about your [PROCESS]"
- "curious about something"

### Email Body

Apply the Voice Profile to every element:

**Opening line** should match their natural opening pattern:
- If they jump right in: "Noticed [COMPANY] just [TRIGGER EVENT]..."
- If they greet first: "Hey [NAME], saw your post about [TOPIC]..."
- If they set context: "I've been working with [ICP TITLES] in [INDUSTRY] and something came up that made me think of [COMPANY]..."

**Body** should match their sentence structure and vocabulary:
- If punchy: short paragraphs, 1-2 sentences each, simple words
- If detailed: more context, but still concise for cold email
- Use their actual vocabulary — if they say "grow" not "scale," write "grow"

**CTA** should match their natural ask style:
- If direct: "Worth a conversation?"
- If soft: "Would it be helpful if I shared how we approached this for [SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE]?"
- If curious: "How are you thinking about [CHALLENGE] right now?"

### LinkedIn DMs

DMs are inherently more casual than email. The Voice Profile should lean toward the user's most casual professional register:

- Match their LinkedIn writing style specifically (which may differ from email)
- Use the punctuation and formatting they use on LinkedIn
- Match the length they typically use in DMs (usually shorter than email)

### Follow-Ups

Follow-up tone should be consistent with the original message:
- If the first email was casual, follow-ups should be casual
- If the user is the type to add humor, a light touch in follow-ups works
- If the user is direct, follow-ups should be direct, not apologetic

**Example — Direct voice follow-up:**
"Hey [NAME] — circling back on my note from last week. Still relevant, or bad timing?"

**Example — Warm voice follow-up:**
"Hi [NAME], just wanted to bump this in case it got buried. No pressure at all — just thought the [SPECIFIC IDEA] might be worth a quick chat."

**Example — Curious voice follow-up:**
"[NAME] — wondering if you had a chance to think about [TOPIC]? Either way, I'd love to hear your take."

## Voice Evolution

Your voice is not static. It evolves as you:
- Get more comfortable with outreach
- Learn what resonates with your audience
- Develop new communication habits
- Adjust to different markets or ICPs

### When to Update the Voice Profile

- **Every 3 months:** Quick review — does this still sound like you?
- **After major feedback:** If multiple prospects comment on your tone (positively or negatively)
- **When changing ICP:** Different audiences may require tone adjustments (enterprise vs. startup)
- **When team members join:** Each person needs their own Voice Profile, not a copy of yours

### How to Update

1. Collect 5-10 new writing samples
2. Compare to existing Voice Profile
3. Note any shifts
4. Update the profile document
5. Review all active templates against the updated profile

## Testing Voice Accuracy

### The Side-by-Side Test

The gold standard for voice accuracy:

1. Take 5 emails the user has written themselves
2. Take 5 emails generated using the Voice Profile
3. Randomize the order
4. Have the user (or someone who knows them well) try to identify which are AI-generated

**Success criteria:** If they can't reliably tell the difference (accuracy below 70%), the Voice Profile is working.

**If they CAN tell the difference:** Ask which ones felt "off" and why. Common reasons:
- Vocabulary that doesn't match ("I would never say 'leverage'")
- Tone is slightly too formal or too casual
- Missing signature phrases
- Sentence structure is too uniform (real writing has more variation)
- Over-use of a pattern that the user only uses occasionally

### The "Read Aloud" Test

Have the user read the AI-generated email aloud. If they stumble, pause, or feel the need to rephrase, those are voice mismatches. Note every point where they'd say it differently.

### The "Would I Actually Send This?" Test

For each AI-generated message, ask: "Would you send this as-is, or would you need to change something?" If they consistently need to change things, the Voice Profile needs refinement. Track what they change — those changes ARE the voice data you need.

## Common Voice Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Being Too Formal When the User Is Casual
**What it looks like:** "I hope this message finds you well. I wanted to reach out regarding..."
**When the user actually writes like:** "Hey — quick thought for you."
**Fix:** Drop the formalities. Match the user's directness. Use contractions. Start mid-thought.

### Mistake 2: Using Jargon the User Never Uses
**What it looks like:** "We help optimize your go-to-market motion and drive scalable pipeline."
**When the user actually writes like:** "We help you find more customers."
**Fix:** Use the "Words they NEVER use" list religiously. Replace every jargon word with the user's actual vocabulary.

### Mistake 3: Being Too Salesy When the User Is Consultative
**What it looks like:** "I'd love to show you how our solution can transform your business."
**When the user actually writes like:** "I've seen a few patterns in your space — want me to share what's working?"
**Fix:** Shift from "selling" language to "sharing" language. Position as a peer, not a vendor.

### Mistake 4: Over-Using a Pattern
**What it looks like:** Every email starts with "Noticed that [COMPANY]..." and ends with "Worth exploring?"
**When the user actually writes:** With variety — different openings, different closings, different structures.
**Fix:** Create 3-4 variants of each pattern and rotate. Real human writing has variation.

### Mistake 5: Missing Emotional Range
**What it looks like:** Every message has the same flat, neutral tone.
**When the user actually writes:** With genuine excitement about some things, concern about others, curiosity about questions.
**Fix:** Map the user's emotional range. When should the copy show enthusiasm? When should it be measured? When should it show empathy?

### Mistake 6: Copying Rather Than Capturing
**What it looks like:** Lifting exact phrases from samples and stitching them together (Franken-voice).
**What it should be:** Understanding the PATTERNS and PRINCIPLES behind their writing, then generating new content that follows those patterns naturally.
**Fix:** Focus on the "why" behind their choices, not just the "what." Why do they use short sentences? Because they value directness. Apply that principle, don't just copy-paste short sentences.

## The Voice Refinement Loop

After initial Voice Profile creation, run this continuous improvement loop:

1. **Generate** outreach copy using the Voice Profile
2. **Review** with the user — mark any phrases/sentences that feel "off"
3. **Analyze** the mismatches — what pattern did the AI miss?
4. **Update** the Voice Profile with new data
5. **Regenerate** and compare — is the new version closer?
6. **Repeat** until the user approves without changes

This loop typically takes 2-3 iterations to get to 90%+ accuracy. Perfect accuracy isn't the goal — "close enough that they'd send it with minimal edits" is the goal.

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering the Voice Profile, verify:

- [ ] At least 10 writing samples were analyzed (more is better)
- [ ] Samples come from multiple sources (not just one channel)
- [ ] Samples represent the user's NATURAL voice (not edited/published content)
- [ ] All 9 voice dimensions have been analyzed with specific observations
- [ ] The "Words they USE" list contains 10+ actual words/phrases from their writing
- [ ] The "Words they NEVER use" list reflects genuine anti-patterns
- [ ] The tone description is nuanced (not just "professional" — HOW professional?)
- [ ] The Voice Summary (3-5 adjectives) is specific and accurate
- [ ] Example rewrites are dramatically different from the generic versions
- [ ] Example rewrites would pass the "Would I actually send this?" test
- [ ] The profile includes what the voice IS and what it IS NOT
- [ ] Channel-specific guidance is provided (email vs. LinkedIn vs. DM)
- [ ] The profile is actionable — someone could use it to generate copy without seeing the original samples
- [ ] The user has been asked to validate the profile and flag any inaccuracies
- [ ] A refinement plan is in place (iterate based on feedback)
