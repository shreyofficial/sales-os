# Connection Request Crafter

## Purpose

Write connection request messages that get accepted at 40-60%+ rates — turning strangers into connections who are open to hearing from you. This skill provides 8 proven formulas, personalization techniques, volume strategy, and A/B testing methods so you build a system, not just a set of one-off messages.

---

Before you begin, confirm you have completed:

- **ICP Architect** — You know exactly who you are targeting (titles, industries, pain points)
- **Offer Positioning Engine** — You have a clear, compelling offer you can reference
- **Prospect List Builder** — You have a qualified, organized list of prospects to connect with

## Part 1: The Science of Connection Requests

### 1.1 Why Connection Requests Matter

The connection request is the gateway to everything else. If your request is not accepted, you cannot:
- Send a DM (unless you use InMail, which is expensive and has lower reply rates)
- Appear in their feed consistently
- Engage with their content in a way they will notice
- Build a relationship over time

A rejected or ignored connection request is a dead end. Getting accepted is the first sale — you are selling the idea that you are worth connecting with.

### 1.2 Average Acceptance Rates (Benchmarks)

| Approach | Typical Acceptance Rate |
|---|---|
| No note (blank request) | 25-35% |
| Generic note ("I'd like to add you to my network") | 15-25% |
| Personalized note (references something specific) | 40-55% |
| Highly personalized + warm signal (engaged with content, mutual connection) | 55-70% |
| Immediate pitch in connection request | 5-15% |

The data is clear: personalization dramatically increases acceptance rates, and pitching in the connection request kills them.

### 1.3 What Kills Acceptance Rates

- Pitching your service in the connection request
- Generic, copy-paste messages with no personalization
- Messages that are clearly automated
- Being overly formal ("Dear Sir/Madam, I would be honored...")
- Being overly casual ("Hey bro! Let's connect!")
- Messages that are all about you and what you do
- Sending from a profile with no photo, sparse information, or a spammy headline
- Requesting connections with people who have zero reason to know or care about you, without giving them a reason

### 1.4 The 300-Character Constraint

LinkedIn connection request notes are limited to 300 characters. Every character counts.

**Optimization principles:**
- Lead with the reason for connecting — do not waste characters on pleasantries
- Use their first name (not full name — saves characters and feels more natural)
- Remove filler words: "just," "really," "I think," "I was wondering if"
- Use contractions: "I'd" instead of "I would," "you're" instead of "you are"
- End with a soft close or implied benefit, not a hard ask
- Do not use emojis excessively — one is fine, three is spammy

## Part 2: The 8 Connection Request Formulas

### Formula 1: Mutual Connection Reference

**When to use:** You share a mutual connection with the prospect, especially if that mutual connection is someone respected in their industry or network.

**Why it works:** Social proof. If someone they know and trust is connected to you, you are implicitly endorsed. It makes you feel familiar, not foreign.

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], I see we're both connected to [Mutual Connection]. I work in [INDUSTRY/SPACE] and would love to be part of your network. [Optional: 1 sentence about what caught your eye on their profile.]
```

**Example 1 — Marketing Agency Owner targeting CMOs:**
```
Hi Sarah, noticed we're both connected to David Chen. I work in B2B marketing and have been following your company's growth — really impressive trajectory. Would love to connect.
```

**Example 2 — IT Services targeting CTOs:**
```
Hi James, we share a few mutual connections in the tech space, including Rachel Kwon. I focus on IT infrastructure for growing companies and thought it'd be great to connect.
```

**Example 3 — Financial Advisor targeting Business Owners:**
```
Hi Michael, I see we're both connected to Lisa Tran. I specialize in financial planning for business owners and would love to add you to my network.
```

---

### Formula 2: Content-Based

**When to use:** The prospect recently posted, shared, or commented on something you can genuinely reference. This is one of the highest-converting formulas because it proves you actually looked at their profile.

**Why it works:** People love knowing their content is being seen and valued. It demonstrates you are not mass-blasting requests.

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], saw your [post/comment/article] about [TOPIC]. [1 sentence of genuine reaction or insight]. Would love to connect and keep learning from your perspective.
```

**Example 1 — Consultant targeting Agency Owners:**
```
Hi Alex, your post about client retention vs. acquisition really resonated — especially the point about lifetime value being 5x more important. Would love to connect.
```

**Example 2 — SaaS Founder targeting HR Directors:**
```
Hi Priya, your article on remote onboarding challenges was spot-on. We're seeing the same trends across our client base. Would love to stay connected.
```

**Example 3 — Sales Trainer targeting VP of Sales:**
```
Hi Marcus, loved your comment on Jake's post about cold call frameworks. Your point about tonality over scripts is something I've seen firsthand. Let's connect.
```

---

### Formula 3: Industry Peer

**When to use:** You operate in the same industry or adjacent space as the prospect. You are positioning yourself as a peer, not a vendor.

**Why it works:** People connect with peers reflexively. There is no threat or sales pressure — just professional networking.

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], fellow [INDUSTRY] professional here. I focus on [YOUR AREA] and always looking to connect with others in the space. Would love to be in your network.
```

**Example 1 — Web Designer targeting other Agency Owners:**
```
Hi Jordan, fellow digital agency owner here. I focus on the design side and love seeing how other agencies approach growth. Would be great to connect.
```

**Example 2 — Recruiter targeting Talent Leaders:**
```
Hi Samantha, I'm in the talent acquisition space as well — focused on tech hiring. Always great to connect with others solving the same problems. Let's connect.
```

**Example 3 — Fractional CFO targeting Startup Founders:**
```
Hi Daniel, I work closely with startup founders on the finance side and noticed we're in similar circles. Would love to be part of your network.
```

---

### Formula 4: Specific Compliment + Shared Interest

**When to use:** You genuinely admire something about their work, company, or profile, and you share a relevant interest or focus area.

**Why it works:** Flattery works — as long as it is specific and genuine. Generic flattery ("impressive profile!") feels hollow. Specific compliments feel authentic.

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], really impressed by [SPECIFIC THING — company milestone, career path, project, initiative]. I'm also focused on [SHARED INTEREST/AREA]. Would love to connect.
```

**Example 1 — Growth Consultant targeting E-Commerce Founders:**
```
Hi Taylor, really impressed by how quickly [COMPANY] scaled to 8 figures. I'm deep in the DTC growth space and would love to follow your journey. Let's connect.
```

**Example 2 — Leadership Coach targeting Executives:**
```
Hi Renee, your career path from engineering to CEO is genuinely inspiring. I'm passionate about leadership development and would love to be in your network.
```

**Example 3 — Cybersecurity Firm targeting CISOs:**
```
Hi David, saw that [COMPANY] just earned SOC 2 compliance — great milestone. I'm in the security space as well and would love to connect.
```

---

### Formula 5: Event/Group-Based

**When to use:** You and the prospect are in the same LinkedIn group, attended the same event (virtual or in-person), or participated in the same webinar.

**Why it works:** Shared experiences create instant common ground. It gives the connection a reason to exist beyond "I want to sell you something."

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], noticed we're both in [GROUP NAME] / both attended [EVENT NAME]. [1 sentence about the event/group]. Would love to connect and continue the conversation.
```

**Example 1 — SaaS Sales targeting Startup Founders:**
```
Hi Omar, saw we're both in the SaaS Growth Tactics group. The discussion on PLG vs. sales-led has been really interesting. Would love to connect.
```

**Example 2 — Marketing Agency targeting CMOs:**
```
Hi Claire, noticed we both attended the MarketingProfs B2B Forum. The session on ABM was outstanding. Would love to stay connected.
```

**Example 3 — Business Coach targeting Entrepreneurs:**
```
Hi Kevin, we're both in the Scaling Up group and I've seen your thoughtful comments. Would love to add you to my network.
```

---

### Formula 6: Value-First

**When to use:** You have a specific, small piece of value you can offer upfront — an insight, a resource, a benchmark, a tip. This works best when the value is genuinely relevant to their role or situation.

**Why it works:** Reciprocity. When you give something first, people naturally want to reciprocate (by accepting, responding, or engaging).

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], I recently [created/found/compiled] a [RESOURCE] on [TOPIC RELEVANT TO THEIR ROLE]. Happy to share if it's useful. Either way, would love to connect.
```

**Example 1 — SEO Agency targeting Marketing Directors:**
```
Hi Aisha, I just put together a quick benchmark report on B2B SaaS SEO trends for 2026. Happy to share if it's useful. Would love to connect regardless.
```

**Example 2 — Sales Consultant targeting VP of Sales:**
```
Hi Brian, I compiled outbound conversion benchmarks across 40+ B2B sales teams. If that's useful I'm happy to send it over. Let's connect.
```

**Example 3 — HR Tech targeting HR Directors:**
```
Hi Megan, I just pulled together data on time-to-hire trends in mid-market companies. Thought it might be relevant to your team. Would love to connect.
```

---

### Formula 7: Direct and Honest

**When to use:** When you do not have a specific hook (no mutual connection, no content to reference, no shared group) but the prospect is a strong ICP match. Honesty can be refreshing in a sea of manufactured rapport.

**Why it works:** Authenticity stands out. When someone is transparent about why they want to connect, it builds trust. This works especially well in industries where people value directness (finance, sales, engineering, etc.).

**Template:**
```
Hi [First Name], I'll be direct — I work with [ICP DESCRIPTION] on [WHAT YOU DO], and your profile stood out. No pitch, just think we'd benefit from being connected. Open to it?
```

**Example 1 — Business Consultant targeting Founders:**
```
Hi Nathan, I'll be upfront — I help service-based founders scale past $1M, and your profile caught my eye. Not pitching, just think we'd have a lot in common. Open to connecting?
```

**Example 2 — Recruiter targeting Engineering Leaders:**
```
Hi Sophie, being direct — I focus on engineering leadership talent and your work at [COMPANY] stood out. Always good to know top people in the space. Okay to connect?
```

**Example 3 — Ad Agency targeting E-Commerce Brands:**
```
Hi Chris, I work with DTC brands on paid media and your brand caught my attention. No agenda — just think it'd be valuable to connect. Sound good?
```

### Formula 8: No-Note Strategy

**When to use:** Counterintuitively, sending NO note with your connection request can sometimes outperform a mediocre note. Consider using the no-note approach when:

- Your profile is very strong (clear headline, professional photo, compelling summary) and speaks for itself
- You are connecting with people who receive dozens of pitchy connection requests and appreciate the simplicity
- You are connecting with people in your immediate industry where the connection is obvious
- You have already engaged with their content (liked, commented) and they may recognize your name/photo

**Why it works:** A blank connection request lets your profile do the talking. There is no risk of a poorly written note turning them off. Many senior executives prefer blank requests because they signal "no sales pitch incoming."

**When NOT to use it:**
- When your profile is not optimized (weak headline, no photo, sparse)
- When connecting with people outside your industry who have no reason to know you
- When you have a strong personalization hook (content, mutual connection) that would significantly boost acceptance

**Best practice:** Test the no-note approach with 20-30 prospects and compare acceptance rates to your best-performing note formula. Use data, not assumptions, to decide.

## Part 3: Personalization Techniques That Scale

Personalization does not have to take 10 minutes per prospect. Here is how to personalize quickly and effectively at scale.

### 3.1 The 30-Second Profile Scan

For each prospect, spend 30 seconds scanning for one of these personalization hooks:

1. **Recent post or comment** — If they posted in the last 2 weeks, reference it. This is the strongest hook.
2. **Headline** — Their headline often reveals what they care about most. Reference it.
3. **Mutual connections** — If you share a mutual connection, mention them.
4. **Company news** — Quick Google search: "[Company Name] news" — look for funding, awards, launches, hires.
5. **Career milestone** — New role, promotion, work anniversary — LinkedIn notifies you of these.

### 3.2 Personalization at Scale Workflow

1. Pull up your active batch (20-30 prospects).
2. Open each profile in a new browser tab.
3. For each, do the 30-second scan and note the best personalization hook in your spreadsheet.
4. Group prospects by the type of hook you found (content-based, mutual connection, company news, etc.).
5. Write one template per hook type.
6. Customize the template for each individual (swap in the specific post, connection name, news item, etc.).

**This process takes 30-45 minutes for a batch of 25 prospects** — approximately 90 seconds per personalized connection request.

### 3.3 Personalization Hooks by Priority

| Priority | Hook Type | Conversion Impact |
|---|---|---|
| 1 (Highest) | Referenced their specific content | Very high |
| 2 | Mutual connection mentioned by name | High |
| 3 | Company news or milestone | High |
| 4 | Shared group or event | Medium-high |
| 5 | Specific compliment about their work | Medium |
| 6 | Industry peer positioning | Medium |
| 7 | Direct and honest (no specific hook) | Medium |
| 8 | No note | Varies |

## Part 4: What NOT to Do

### 4.1 The Immediate Pitch

**Never do this:**
```
Hi [Name], I help [ICP] achieve [RESULT] through [SERVICE]. Would love to set up a quick call to show you how. When works for you?
```

This is the number one reason people reject connection requests. You are asking for a meeting before you have even been accepted as a connection. It signals that you see them as a transaction, not a person.

### 4.2 The Generic Template

**Never do this:**
```
Hi [Name], I'd like to add you to my professional network on LinkedIn.
```

This is LinkedIn's default text. Sending it tells the prospect you did not care enough to write anything. Ironically, sending NO note at all often outperforms this — because at least a blank request does not feel lazy.

### 4.3 Too Casual

**Never do this:**
```
Hey! Saw your profile and you seem cool. Let's connect and chat sometime! 🔥🔥
```

This is not a text to a friend. It reads as unprofessional and, depending on context, can feel inappropriate.

### 4.4 Too Formal

**Never do this:**
```
Dear Mr. Johnson, I would be honored to connect with you on this professional networking platform. Your distinguished career is commendable.
```

This reads like a cover letter from 2005. LinkedIn is professional but conversational.

### 4.5 The Autobiography

**Never do this:**
```
Hi [Name], I'm the founder of [Company], a leading provider of [Service] with 15 years of experience helping [ICP] achieve [Result]. We've worked with [Client A], [Client B], and [Client C]...
```

The connection request is not about you. It is about why the connection makes sense for THEM.

## Part 5: A/B Testing Connection Request Messages

### 5.1 How to Set Up A/B Tests

1. Choose 2 formulas to test (e.g., Content-Based vs. Direct and Honest).
2. Send Formula A to 15-20 prospects in one batch.
3. Send Formula B to 15-20 prospects in another batch (matched by ICP characteristics).
4. Wait 7 days.
5. Compare acceptance rates.

### 5.2 What to Track

| Metric | Formula A | Formula B |
|---|---|---|
| Requests Sent | | |
| Requests Accepted | | |
| Acceptance Rate (%) | | |
| Replies Received After Accepting | | |
| Reply Rate (%) | | |

**Important:** Track not just acceptance rate but reply rate after acceptance. A formula that gets 50% acceptance but 5% replies is worse than one that gets 40% acceptance but 20% replies.

### 5.3 Test Cadence

- Run one A/B test every 2 weeks.
- Always keep your current best-performing formula as "Control" and test a new variation as "Challenger."
- After 4-6 weeks, you will have a clear winning formula for your specific ICP.

## Part 6: Volume Strategy

### 6.1 Daily and Weekly Limits

LinkedIn limits the number of connection requests you can send per week to prevent spam. The exact limits vary, but here are safe guidelines:

| Account Type | Daily Safe Limit | Weekly Safe Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Free LinkedIn | 10-15 requests/day | 50-70 requests/week |
| LinkedIn Premium / Sales Navigator | 15-25 requests/day | 80-100 requests/week |

> **Warning:** Exceeding these limits can result in a temporary restriction on your ability to send connection requests. If LinkedIn warns you, stop immediately and wait 24-48 hours.

### 6.2 Timing

- **Best days:** Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- **Best times:** 8-10 AM and 5-7 PM in the prospect's local timezone
- **Avoid:** Weekends, Mondays (inbox overload), Fridays (checked out)
- **Spread requests throughout the day** — do not send 20 requests in 5 minutes. Send 5-7 in the morning, 5-7 in the afternoon.

### 6.3 Weekly Outreach Rhythm

| Day | Activity |
|---|---|
| Monday | Plan the week's batch, research personalization hooks |
| Tuesday | Send 15-20 connection requests (Batch A) |
| Wednesday | Send 10-15 connection requests (Batch B), DM new acceptances from Tuesday |
| Thursday | DM new acceptances from Wednesday, engage with prospect content |
| Friday | DM remaining acceptances, review weekly metrics, plan next batch |
