# Voice Note Scripter

## Purpose

Create word-for-word voice note scripts for LinkedIn DMs that get 2-3x higher reply rates than text messages. Voice notes are one of the most underused and highest-performing outreach tools on LinkedIn. This skill gives you 8 ready-to-record scripts, delivery techniques, and a strategy for when and how to use them.

- **DM Opener Generator** — You understand the principles of first-touch messaging
- **ICP Architect** — You know who you are talking to and what they care about

## Part 1: Why Voice Notes Work

### 1.1 The Data

Voice notes on LinkedIn consistently outperform text-based DMs:

| Metric | Text DM | Voice Note DM |
|---|---|---|
| Open rate | 40-60% | 70-85% |
| Reply rate | 8-15% | 20-40% |
| Positive sentiment in replies | Moderate | High |
| Perceived effort by recipient | Low | High |

### 1.2 The Psychology

**Pattern interrupt.** The vast majority of LinkedIn messages are text. A voice note immediately stands out in a sea of typed messages. The recipient sees the audio waveform and thinks "this is different."

**Personal connection.** Hearing someone's voice creates a fundamentally different connection than reading their words. Tone, warmth, enthusiasm, and personality come through in ways that text cannot convey.

**Trust building.** A voice note signals that you are a real person, not a bot or automation tool. It takes effort to record a voice note — and that effort is perceived as a sign of genuine interest.

**Harder to ignore.** People feel a psychological pull to listen to a voice note that they do not feel with text. Leaving a voice note unplayed creates an "open loop" that many people feel compelled to close.

**Curiosity.** There is something inherently intriguing about an unplayed voice message. People want to know what was said.

### 1.3 When to Use Voice Notes

| Situation | Use Voice Note? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Post-connection acceptance (first DM) | Yes — strong | Sets you apart immediately from every other connection who sends text |
| Cold outreach opener (to an existing connection) | Yes — strong | Pattern interrupt, builds instant rapport |
| Follow-up after no reply to text DM | Yes — very strong | Breaks through where text failed; shows extra effort |
| After they engaged with your content | Yes — strong | Creates a personal, memorable follow-up |
| Re-engagement after "not now" | Yes — strong | Fresh format, renewed effort |
| Deep into a text conversation | Sometimes | Can feel jarring if the conversation has been text-based; use selectively |
| Sending to someone who has NOT accepted your connection | No — except via InMail | You cannot send voice notes to non-connections |
| Responding to a detailed question about your service | Depends | Sometimes text is better for detailed/technical info they may want to reference |

## Part 2: The Ideal Voice Note

### 2.1 Length

| Duration | Rating | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| 15-30 seconds | Good | Quick follow-up, check-in, simple opener |
| 30-60 seconds | Ideal | Full opener, value drop, case study mention |
| 60-90 seconds | Maximum | Only if you are sharing something genuinely detailed and valuable |
| 90+ seconds | Too long | Never. The recipient will tune out or not listen at all. |

**The sweet spot is 30-45 seconds.** Long enough to say something meaningful, short enough to respect their time.

### 2.2 Structure Framework

Every voice note should follow this structure:

```
[Greeting + Name]
[1 sentence: Context — why you're reaching out]
[1-2 sentences: Value — insight, compliment, or observation]
[1 sentence: Question or soft CTA]
```

Total: 3-4 sentences, 30-45 seconds.

### 2.3 What to Avoid

- Rambling or stream-of-consciousness speaking
- Reading a script word-for-word in a robotic monotone
- Starting with "Uhh" or "Sooo" — start clean
- Going over 60 seconds
- Pitching your service directly
- Sounding out of breath, distracted, or like you are in a noisy environment
- Sending voice notes back-to-back (one is enough per touch point)

## Part 3: The 8 Voice Note Scripts

### Script 1: Post-Connection Acceptance Opener

**When to use:** Within 24-48 hours of someone accepting your connection request. This is the highest-converting voice note scenario because the connection is fresh.

**Duration:** 30-40 seconds

**Script (read naturally, not word-for-word robotically):**

```
"Hey [Name], thanks for connecting — I really appreciate it. I came across your profile because [SPECIFIC REASON — their content, mutual connection, LinkedIn search in their industry] and was genuinely impressed by [SPECIFIC OBSERVATION about their work or company]. I'd love to learn more about what you're working on at [COMPANY]. What's the biggest thing on your plate right now?"
```

**Tone guidance:** Warm, friendly, genuinely curious. Imagine you just met this person at a casual industry event and are starting a conversation.

**Timing:** Send within 24 hours of connection acceptance. The longer you wait, the colder the connection becomes.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Sarah, thanks for connecting — really appreciate it. I came across your profile while researching B2B marketing leaders in the SaaS space and was genuinely impressed by the content strategy you've built at [COMPANY]. I'd love to learn more about what you're focused on right now. What's the biggest marketing challenge on your plate this quarter?"
```

### Script 2: Cold Outreach Opener

**When to use:** As your first DM to an existing connection you have not messaged before (maybe you connected weeks or months ago).

**Duration:** 35-45 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], hope you're having a great [day of week]. We've been connected for a while and I realized I never actually reached out — which is a miss on my part because I find the work you're doing at [COMPANY] really interesting. Specifically, [1 SPECIFIC OBSERVATION about their company, a recent post, or their role]. I'm curious — [QUESTION RELATED TO THEIR WORK OR CHALLENGES]. Would love to hear your take."
```

**Tone guidance:** Conversational, slightly apologetic for not reaching out sooner (this is endearing, not weak). Genuine curiosity.

**Timing:** Send Tuesday-Thursday, between 9-11 AM in their timezone.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Marcus, hope you're having a great Wednesday. We've been connected for a while and I realized I never actually reached out — which is a miss on my part because I find what you're doing on the sales enablement side at [COMPANY] really interesting. Specifically, the team growth you've had this year is impressive. I'm curious — with scaling that fast, how are you keeping the new reps ramping up to speed? Would love to hear your take."
```

### Script 3: Content Engagement Follow-Up

**When to use:** When the prospect recently posted or engaged with content on LinkedIn, and you want to use that as an entry point.

**Duration:** 30-40 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], I just saw your [post / comment / article] about [TOPIC] and it really resonated with me — especially the point about [SPECIFIC DETAIL FROM THEIR CONTENT]. I've been seeing something similar in [YOUR CONTEXT], and it made me curious about your experience. [FOLLOW-UP QUESTION ABOUT THE TOPIC]. Would love to hear your thoughts."
```

**Tone guidance:** Enthusiastic but not over-the-top. You are a thoughtful peer who was genuinely struck by their content.

**Timing:** Within 48 hours of their content being published. Relevance decays quickly.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Jordan, I just saw your post about why agencies should fire bad-fit clients — and it really resonated with me, especially the point about how one difficult client can drain the energy of the whole team. I've been seeing something similar across the agencies I talk to. Curious — how do you decide when it's time to part ways with a client? Would love to hear your process."
```

---

### Script 4: Value Drop with Insight

**When to use:** When you have a specific, relevant insight, stat, or piece of knowledge that would be genuinely useful to the prospect.

**Duration:** 40-50 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], quick thought I wanted to share with you. I was recently [looking at data / working with a client / reading a report] on [TOPIC RELEVANT TO THEIR ROLE], and something really stood out — [1-2 SENTENCE INSIGHT]. The reason I thought of you is because [HOW IT CONNECTS TO THEIR SITUATION AT COMPANY]. I'm curious whether that matches what you're seeing on your end. Either way, thought it was worth passing along."
```

**Tone guidance:** Knowledgeable but not preachy. You are sharing something genuinely useful, not lecturing them.

**Timing:** Any time — this is a value-forward message that does not depend on a trigger event.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Priya, quick thought I wanted to share with you. I was recently looking at data on cloud migration timelines for mid-market companies, and something really stood out — companies that did phased migrations over 6-9 months had 60% fewer security incidents than those that tried to do it all at once. The reason I thought of you is because of [COMPANY]'s recent cloud initiative. I'm curious whether that matches your experience. Either way, thought it was worth mentioning."
```

### Script 5: Case Study Mention

**When to use:** When you have a relevant success story with a similar company or person that would create curiosity without being a direct pitch.

**Duration:** 40-50 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], I wanted to share a quick story that made me think of you. We recently worked with a [SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE — same industry, same size, same role] that was dealing with [CHALLENGE]. Long story short, they [ACHIEVED SPECIFIC RESULT] in [TIMEFRAME] by [HIGH-LEVEL APPROACH]. The reason it made me think of [COMPANY] is [1 SENTENCE CONNECTING IT TO THEIR SITUATION]. I'm not sure if it's relevant to you right now, but if it is, I'd love to compare notes. Either way, hope you're well."
```

**Tone guidance:** Storytelling mode — natural, conversational, like you are telling a friend about something interesting that happened. Not a pitch.

**Timing:** Use as Touch 3 in your sequence (Day 7-8) or as a standalone outreach message.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey David, wanted to share a quick story that made me think of you. We recently worked with a manufacturing company — similar size to [COMPANY] — that was dealing with cash flow visibility issues. Long story short, they went from guessing their monthly numbers to having a real-time financial dashboard in about 6 weeks. The reason it made me think of [COMPANY] is that I noticed you're in a similar growth phase where financial clarity becomes really critical. Not sure if it's relevant right now, but if it is, I'd love to compare notes."
```

### Script 6: Check-In After Silence

**When to use:** When you sent a text DM and got no reply. The voice note serves as a pattern interrupt that breaks through where text failed.

**Duration:** 25-35 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], I sent you a message a few days ago and wanted to follow up — but rather than send another text that gets lost in the shuffle, figured a voice note might be easier. [1 SENTENCE RECAP OF YOUR PREVIOUS MESSAGE'S CORE POINT — not "I sent a message about..."]. No pressure at all — just curious if that resonated with you. Either way, hope you're having a great week."
```

**Tone guidance:** Light, casual, zero pressure. You are not frustrated or annoyed by the silence — you are understanding and offering a different format.

**Timing:** 3-5 days after the unanswered text DM.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Emily, I dropped you a note a few days ago about some trends I'm seeing in DTC acquisition costs and figured a voice note might cut through better than another text. Basically, I'm seeing some really interesting shifts in how top-performing brands are structuring their paid media, and given what you're building at [BRAND], I thought you might find it useful. No pressure at all — just curious if it's something on your radar."
```

### Script 7: Event/News-Based

**When to use:** When there is a timely trigger — the prospect's company announced something, they spoke at an event, their industry had a major development, or a relevant conference just happened.

**Duration:** 35-45 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], I just [saw the news about / attended / heard about] [SPECIFIC EVENT OR NEWS]. [1 SENTENCE REACTION — congratulations, a genuine observation, or a thoughtful question]. I imagine that's going to have a big impact on [ASPECT OF THEIR ROLE OR COMPANY]. I'd love to hear your perspective on how you're thinking about [RELATED TOPIC]. Congrats again / looking forward to hearing your thoughts."
```

**Tone guidance:** Timely, enthusiastic, genuine. The voice note should feel like a natural reaction to something that just happened, not a calculated outreach move.

**Timing:** Within 48 hours of the trigger event. Relevance is everything here — the faster you send it, the more natural it feels.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Lisa, I just saw the announcement about [COMPANY] raising a Series B — huge congrats, that's a massive milestone. I imagine the next 6-12 months are going to be all about scaling the team and the systems to match. I'd love to hear how you're thinking about building out the people ops side as you grow. Either way, congratulations again — well deserved."
```

### Script 8: Re-Engagement After "Not Now"

**When to use:** When a prospect previously said "not now," "not the right time," or similar, and enough time has passed (60-90 days) that it is appropriate to re-engage.

**Duration:** 30-40 seconds

**Script:**

```
"Hey [Name], it's been a little while since we last chatted — you mentioned back in [MONTH] that the timing wasn't quite right for [TOPIC]. I wanted to circle back because [1 SENTENCE ABOUT WHAT'S NEW — a new insight, a new result, a shift in the market, a new resource you created]. Figured it might be worth a quick conversation now that some time has passed. No pressure at all — but if you're open to it, I'm here. Hope things are going well at [COMPANY]."
```

**Tone guidance:** Respectful of their previous boundary, but confident that you have something new and relevant to share. Not apologetic — matter-of-fact and warm.

**Timing:** 60-90 days after the "not now" response. Check your "Not Now" pipeline for the exact date.

**Example filled in:**
```
"Hey Omar, it's been about three months since we last chatted — you mentioned back in January that cybersecurity training wasn't a top priority at the time. I wanted to circle back because we just published some new research on AI-powered phishing attacks that's gotten a lot of attention in your space — and the data is pretty alarming. Figured it might be worth a quick conversation now. No pressure at all, but if you're open to it, I'd love to share what we're seeing. Hope things are going well."
```

## Part 4: Delivery Tips

### 4.1 How to Sound Natural (Not Robotic)

- **Do not read word-for-word.** Use the scripts as guides, not teleprompter scripts. Familiarize yourself with the structure and key points, then speak naturally.
- **Use their name.** Saying someone's name creates instant attention and personal connection.
- **Smile while recording.** This sounds silly, but smiling physically changes your vocal tone — it adds warmth and friendliness that the listener can hear.
- **Pause briefly before and after.** Start with a one-second pause (avoids cutting off the beginning). End with a one-second pause before stopping the recording (avoids an abrupt cutoff).
- **Speak at a slightly slower pace than normal.** Voice notes can feel rushed. Slowing down by about 10% makes you sound more composed and confident.
- **Vary your tone.** Monotone voice notes are just as boring as monotone text. Emphasize key words, vary your pitch, and let natural enthusiasm come through.
- **Record in a quiet environment.** Background noise is distracting and unprofessional. Find a quiet room. Close the door.

### 4.2 Technical Tips

- Hold the phone 6-8 inches from your mouth
- Use earbuds/AirPods with a built-in mic for cleaner audio
- Test your recording by playing it back before sending (LinkedIn allows this)
- If you stumble, re-record — do not send a voice note with "uh, sorry, let me start over"
- LinkedIn voice notes can be up to 60 seconds on mobile. Desktop does not support voice notes — you must use the mobile app.

### 4.3 The Re-Record Rule

Give yourself a maximum of 3 attempts per voice note. After 3 takes, send whichever was best. Perfection is the enemy of outreach. A "good enough" voice note sent today beats a "perfect" one you never send.

## Part 5: Combining Voice Notes with Text

### 5.1 When to Pair Them

Sometimes a voice note works best when accompanied by a short text message. Here is when and how:

**Scenario 1: Voice note + text follow-up (different days)**
- Day 1: Send a voice note as your opener
- Day 3-4: Follow up with a short text DM that references the voice note: "Hey [Name], not sure if you caught my voice note from the other day — the main point was [1 sentence]. Thoughts?"

**Scenario 2: Text + voice note (same day)**
- Send a brief text DM
- Immediately follow with a voice note: "Also recorded a quick voice note to explain what I mean — easier than typing it all out."
- This works well when sharing something nuanced that benefits from vocal delivery

**Scenario 3: Voice note replacing a text follow-up**
- If your last 2 messages were text and got no reply, switch to a voice note for the next touch
- The format change alone can generate a response

### 5.2 When NOT to Pair Them

- Do not send a voice note and a long text message simultaneously — it feels overwhelming
- Do not send multiple voice notes in a row without a response
- Do not follow up a voice note with "Did you listen to my voice note?" — let it breathe

## Part 6: Voice Note Etiquette

### 6.1 Key Rules

1. **Never send a voice note to someone who has not accepted your connection request.** You cannot technically do this (LinkedIn restricts it), but this also applies to InMail — voice notes via InMail can feel invasive.

2. **One voice note per touch point.** Do not send 3 voice notes in a row. One voice note, wait for a response, then adjust.

3. **Respect cultural norms.** In some cultures and industries, voice notes feel too casual or intimate for professional communication. If you are targeting a highly formal industry (law, finance, government), test voice notes with a small sample before scaling.

4. **Keep it professional.** Friendly and warm, but professional. Avoid slang, inside jokes, or overly casual language — especially in the first message.

5. **Do not assume they will listen.** Some people prefer text and skip voice notes entirely. If you send a voice note and get no reply, follow up with a text version of the same message in your next touch.
