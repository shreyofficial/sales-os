# DM Follow-Up Sequencer

## Purpose

Build multi-touch DM follow-up sequences that convert silent connections into booked calls. Most prospects will not reply to your first message — this is normal. The follow-up sequence is where the real results come from. This skill gives you a complete 7-touch framework with branching logic, timing, reply handling, and tracking systems.

## Part 1: Why Follow-Up Is Where the Money Is

### 1.1 The Follow-Up Data

| Statistic | Source Context |
|---|---|
| 80% of sales require 5+ contacts | Industry-wide B2B sales data |
| 44% of salespeople give up after 1 follow-up | Sales engagement research |
| Only 8% of salespeople follow up 5+ times | Same research |
| Response rates increase by 25% with each additional follow-up (up to touch 5) | LinkedIn outreach studies |

**The math is simple:** Most of your competitors quit after 1-2 messages. If you follow up 5-7 times with VALUE (not "just checking in"), you are in the top 8% of outreach efforts.

### 1.2 Why People Don't Reply to Message 1

Before you take silence personally, understand the common reasons for no reply:

- They were busy when your message arrived and forgot to come back to it
- They saw it but did not feel compelled enough to respond (your message was fine, just not urgent)
- They are not active on LinkedIn messaging (they check it sporadically)
- They meant to reply but got distracted
- They are not currently in buying mode but might be later
- Your message landed in "Other" inbox instead of "Focused"
- They simply did not see it

**Notice:** Only a tiny percentage of non-replies are because they actively do not want to hear from you. Most are passive non-responses, which means follow-up has a real chance of getting through.

### 1.3 The "Different Angle Per Touch" Principle

**Never send a message that says:**
- "Just following up on my last message"
- "Bumping this to the top of your inbox"
- "Did you see my previous message?"
- "Circling back on this"

These messages are lazy, annoying, and do not give the prospect any new reason to respond. Every follow-up must provide a NEW angle, NEW value, or NEW reason to engage.

**Each touch should stand alone.** If the prospect only ever reads Touch 4, it should still make sense and be compelling on its own.

## Part 2: The Complete 7-Touch DM Sequence Framework

### Touch 1 — The Opener (Day 1)

**Goal:** Start a conversation.

This comes from the DM Opener Generator. Use whichever framework fits the prospect. Reference the previous skill for templates.

**Timing:** Day 1 of the sequence.

---

### Touch 2 — The Value Add (Day 3-4)

**Goal:** Provide something useful that demonstrates expertise and relevance.

**Psychology:** You are not following up — you are sharing something valuable. This reframes you from "person who wants something from me" to "person who is giving me something."

**Structure:** [Brief reference to your last message — do NOT just say "following up"] + [Share a resource, insight, or piece of content relevant to their role/industry] + [Soft question or open door]

**Template A — Insight Share:**
```
Hi [Name], I came across something I thought might be relevant to you — [1-2 sentence insight about a trend, stat, or shift in their industry]. Figured it was worth sharing since [brief reason this applies to them]. Thoughts?
```

**Template B — Resource Share:**
```
Hi [Name], I just put together a [quick guide / framework / checklist] on [TOPIC RELEVANT TO THEIR ROLE]. No strings attached — happy to send it over if you're interested. Let me know.
```

**Template C — Article/Content Share:**
```
Hi [Name], just read an article on [TOPIC] that made me think of the work you're doing at [COMPANY]. Key takeaway was [1-sentence summary]. Worth a quick read — want me to send the link?
```

**Example — Marketing Agency targeting CMOs:**
```
Hi Sarah, I noticed something interesting when analyzing B2B content trends this quarter — companies that shifted from gated whitepapers to ungated thought leadership saw a 40% increase in qualified inbound. Given [COMPANY]'s content approach, thought this might be worth exploring. Curious what your take is?
```

**Example — Sales Consultant targeting VP of Sales:**
```
Hi Marcus, I just finished a breakdown of how the top 10% of B2B sales teams are structuring their outbound sequences in 2026. Some surprising data in there. Want me to send it over? No pitch — just thought you might find it useful.
```

**Example — IT Services targeting CTOs:**
```
Hi Priya, just read Gartner's latest on hybrid cloud security challenges — the stat about misconfiguration being the #1 breach vector caught my eye. Seems especially relevant given [COMPANY]'s recent migration. What's your team's approach to config management?
```

### Touch 3 — The Social Proof (Day 7-8)

**Goal:** Build credibility by referencing a result you achieved for someone in a similar situation.

**Psychology:** Social proof reduces risk perception. If someone similar to them got results, they can envision getting results too. This is not a pitch — it is a story.

**Structure:** [Brief anecdote about a similar company/person] + [Specific result] + [Question connecting it to their situation]

**Template:**
```
Hi [Name], wanted to share something quick — we recently worked with a [SIMILAR COMPANY TYPE/TITLE] who was dealing with [CHALLENGE]. They ended up [SPECIFIC RESULT] by [HIGH-LEVEL APPROACH]. It reminded me of [COMPANY]'s situation. Is [CHALLENGE] something you're actively thinking about?
```

**Example — Growth Agency targeting E-Commerce Founders:**
```
Hi Jake, quick story — a DTC brand in the home goods space came to us plateaued at $80K/month in revenue. Within 6 months they hit $250K/month, mainly by restructuring their paid acquisition funnel and dialing in their email flows. Curious — what's the biggest growth lever you're focused on at [BRAND] right now?
```

**Example — HR Tech targeting VP of People:**
```
Hi Maria, thought you'd find this interesting — a company around [COMPANY]'s size cut their time-to-hire by 40% just by restructuring their interview process (not adding more tools). Is hiring speed something you're working on?
```

**Example — Financial Advisor targeting Business Owners:**
```
Hi David, a manufacturing company owner I work with recently saved $180K in taxes by restructuring how he took distributions. He'd been leaving that money on the table for years without realizing it. Is tax optimization something you've been thinking about?
```

### Touch 4 — The Question (Day 12-14)

**Goal:** Re-engage with a thought-provoking question that invites them to share their perspective.

**Psychology:** By this point, if they have seen your messages, they know you are a real person who provides value and has relevant experience. A well-crafted question can be the one that finally prompts a reply — people love sharing their opinions.

**Structure:** [Question about a challenge, trend, or decision relevant to their role] + [Brief context for why you're asking]

**Template A — Challenge Question:**
```
Hi [Name], curious about something — what would you say is the single biggest challenge [ICP TITLE]s face when it comes to [AREA RELATED TO YOUR SERVICE]? I'm doing some research and your perspective would be really valuable.
```

**Template B — Trend Question:**
```
Hi [Name], quick question — with [INDUSTRY TREND] picking up momentum, how are you thinking about its impact on [THEIR AREA OF RESPONSIBILITY]? Would love to hear your take.
```

**Template C — Priority Question:**
```
Hi [Name], if you could fix one thing about [AREA RELATED TO YOUR SERVICE] at [COMPANY] tomorrow, what would it be? Always curious what's top of mind for [ICP TITLE]s.
```

**Example — Leadership Coach targeting CEOs:**
```
Hi Angela, curious — what's the biggest leadership challenge you're navigating right now at [COMPANY]? I ask because I'm seeing a pattern among CEOs at your stage and want to make sure what I'm hearing matches reality.
```

**Example — Marketing Agency targeting Founders:**
```
Hi Leah, quick question — if you could snap your fingers and fix one part of [COMPANY]'s marketing machine tomorrow, what would it be? I'm always curious what founder-operators are most focused on.
```

**Example — Cybersecurity Firm targeting CISOs:**
```
Hi Omar, with AI-driven threats evolving so quickly, what's keeping you up at night from a security standpoint? I'm doing some research on the biggest blind spots for mid-market security teams and would value your perspective.
```

### Touch 5 — The Direct Ask (Day 18-21)

**Goal:** Make a soft but clear CTA to have a conversation — a call, a video chat, or even a longer voice note exchange.

**Psychology:** By touch 5, you have provided value, shared social proof, asked thoughtful questions, and demonstrated expertise. You have earned the right to ask for a conversation. This is not a hard sell — it is a natural next step.

**Structure:** [Acknowledge the conversation so far (or lack thereof)] + [Clear, low-pressure CTA] + [Make it easy to say yes]

**Template A — The Soft Ask:**
```
Hi [Name], I've been sharing a few things that I thought might be relevant to you — and I realize DMs can only go so far. Would you be open to a quick 15-minute conversation? No pitch — just want to learn more about what you're working on and see if there's anything I can help with. Happy to work around your schedule.
```

**Template B — The Specific Ask:**
```
Hi [Name], based on what I know about [COMPANY], I have a few specific ideas on [TOPIC] that I think could move the needle for you. Easier to walk through them live than in a DM. Would you be open to a quick call this week or next? Here's my calendar if it's easier: [LINK]
```

**Template C — The Low-Commitment Ask:**
```
Hi [Name], totally understand if you've been busy. Here's a no-pressure offer: I'm setting aside a few 15-minute time slots this week specifically for conversations with [ICP TITLE]s about [TOPIC]. No agenda, no pitch — just a quick chat. Worth it?
```

**Example — Sales Consultant targeting VP of Sales:**
```
Hi Marcus, I've shared a few things about outbound optimization over the past couple weeks — and I realize this is probably a bigger conversation than DMs can handle. Would you be open to a quick 15-minute chat? I'd love to hear how things are going at [COMPANY] and share a couple specific ideas I have. Totally low pressure — here's my calendar: [LINK]
```

### Touch 6 — The Breakup (Day 28-30)

**Goal:** Final message in the sequence. Create urgency through scarcity (you are moving on) while leaving the door open.

**Psychology:** The "breakup" message works because of loss aversion — people do not want to lose the option to respond, even if they have not used it. It also relieves pressure by making clear you are not going to keep messaging them.

**Structure:** [Acknowledge you've reached out a few times] + [No guilt or passive aggression] + [Leave the door open with a clear, easy way to re-engage]

**Template A — The Classic Breakup:**
```
Hi [Name], I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back — totally get it, you're busy and this may not be a priority right now. I'll leave you in peace. If [TOPIC/CHALLENGE] ever moves up the priority list, feel free to reach out anytime. Wishing you and [COMPANY] all the best.
```

**Template B — The "One Last Thing" Breakup:**
```
Hi [Name], last note from me — I don't want to be that person who keeps messaging. Before I go, wanted to leave you with this: [ONE FINAL PIECE OF VALUE — a stat, an insight, a resource]. If it's ever useful, you know where to find me. All the best with [COMPANY].
```

**Template C — The Casual Breakup:**
```
Hi [Name], I'll take the hint! No hard feelings at all. If [CHALLENGE/TOPIC] ever comes across your desk, my door's always open. Best of luck with everything at [COMPANY].
```

**Important:** Never be passive-aggressive, guilt-tripping, or sarcastic in the breakup message. It should be genuinely warm and professional. Many prospects have responded to breakup messages weeks or months later when the timing was right.

---

### Touch 7 — The Re-Engage (Day 45-60)

**Goal:** Come back after a significant gap with fresh value or news — something new that justifies the re-engagement.

**Psychology:** Time changes circumstances. The prospect who was not interested 45 days ago may now be facing the exact challenge you solve. A fresh, valuable message with a new angle can reignite the conversation.

**Structure:** [Acknowledge the gap naturally] + [Share something new and relevant — a new trend, new result, new resource, new event] + [Soft re-open]

**Template:**
```
Hi [Name], it's been a while — hope things are going well at [COMPANY]. I came across [NEW PIECE OF VALUE/NEWS/TREND] and it made me think of you. [1-2 sentences about why it's relevant to them]. Figured it was worth sharing. Any interest in comparing notes?
```

**Example — Marketing Agency targeting CMOs:**
```
Hi Sarah, it's been a bit — hope Q2 is off to a strong start at [COMPANY]. I just published some new research on B2B content ROI that's gotten a lot of traction. The data surprised even me. Thought you'd find it interesting — want me to send it over?
```

**Example — Sales Consultant targeting VP of Sales:**
```
Hi Marcus, circling back with something fresh — we just ran an analysis of outbound conversion rates across 50 B2B teams and the results are eye-opening. The top-performing teams are doing 3 things differently. Happy to share the data if you're interested.
```

**Timing:** Touch 7 is a soft restart. If they reply, treat it as the beginning of a new conversation (do not immediately pitch). If they do not reply, archive them and revisit in 6 months.

## Part 3: Branching Logic — Handling Different Response Types

Not every prospect will follow the linear sequence. When someone replies, the sequence pauses and you enter a conversation. Here is how to handle different response types:

### Branch A: Positive Response

**Signals:** They reply with interest, ask a question about your work, share information about their situation, or express openness.

**Action:**
1. Reply within 2-4 hours (same business day if possible)
2. Acknowledge what they shared
3. Ask a follow-up question to deepen the conversation
4. Do NOT jump to a pitch — earn 1-2 more message exchanges before transitioning to a call (see Conversation-to-Call Converter)

**Example Reply:**
```
Really appreciate you sharing that, [Name]. That's actually a challenge I hear a lot from [ICP TITLE]s — especially around [SPECIFIC ASPECT THEY MENTIONED]. What have you tried so far to address it?
```

### Branch B: Neutral Response

**Signals:** Short, non-committal replies like "Thanks," "Interesting," "Makes sense," or answering your question without elaboration.

**Action:**
1. Reply with a follow-up question that invites more detail
2. Make the question easy to answer (not requiring much thought or effort)
3. If still neutral after 2 exchanges, try a more direct approach or skip to Touch 5

**Example Reply:**
```
Totally. Curious — is that something you're actively working on right now, or more of a back-burner priority? No wrong answer — just helps me know if there's anything specific I can share that'd be useful.
```

### Branch C: Negative Response

**Signals:** "Not interested," "Please stop messaging me," "We're all set," or any other clear rejection.

**Action:**
1. Reply graciously within the hour
2. Thank them for their time
3. Do NOT push back or try to overcome the objection in this moment
4. Move them to your "Not Now" or "Archive" list
5. Consider revisiting in 6-12 months with a fresh, value-led approach

**Example Reply:**
```
Totally understand, [Name] — appreciate you letting me know. If anything changes down the road, my door's always open. Wishing you and [COMPANY] the best.
```

### Branch D: Question Response

**Signals:** They reply with a question — "What do you do exactly?", "How does that work?", "What would that look like?"

**Action:**
1. This is a buying signal — they are interested enough to want more information
2. Reply with a concise, value-focused answer (not a sales deck)
3. Immediately follow with a question or transition to a call

**Example Reply:**
```
Great question. In short, we help [ICP DESCRIPTION] [ACHIEVE RESULT] by [HIGH-LEVEL APPROACH]. For example, we recently helped a [SIMILAR COMPANY] go from [BEFORE STATE] to [AFTER STATE] in [TIMEFRAME]. The specifics vary depending on the situation — would it be worth hopping on a quick call so I can understand your setup and see if it makes sense?
```

### Branch E: Ghosting (Mid-Conversation)

**Signals:** They replied once or twice but then stopped responding.

**Action:**
1. Wait 3-5 days after their last reply
2. Send a low-pressure follow-up that acknowledges the gap without being needy
3. If no reply, continue the sequence from the next touch

**Example Reply:**
```
Hi [Name], know how it goes — things get busy. Just wanted to circle back on our conversation. No rush at all — whenever you have a moment, I'd love to pick up where we left off. If the timing isn't right, totally understand.
```

## Part 4: Sequence Pacing — Why Timing Matters

### 4.1 Why These Specific Intervals?

| Interval | Reasoning |
|---|---|
| Day 1 → Day 3-4 | Close enough to still be relevant, far enough to not feel aggressive |
| Day 3-4 → Day 7-8 | Gives them a full week to reply to your first two messages |
| Day 7-8 → Day 12-14 | Longer gap signals patience, not desperation |
| Day 12-14 → Day 18-21 | The ask comes after multiple value touches — earned, not forced |
| Day 18-21 → Day 28-30 | Final push with enough space that it does not feel like harassment |
| Day 28-30 → Day 45-60 | Long gap creates freshness for re-engagement |

### 4.2 When to Compress the Timeline

Compress (shorter intervals) when:
- The prospect showed initial engagement (viewed your profile, liked your post)
- There is a time-sensitive trigger (end of quarter, upcoming event, regulatory deadline)
- The prospect is in a fast-moving industry where delays lose relevance

### 4.3 When to Extend the Timeline

Extend (longer intervals) when:
- The prospect is a senior executive (C-suite) — they move slower
- The buying cycle for your service is long (6+ months)
- The prospect's industry has slower decision-making processes (healthcare, government, education)

## Part 5: Tracking Sequence Progress

### 5.1 Simple Spreadsheet Framework

If you do not have a CRM, track sequences with a spreadsheet:

| Column | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Prospect Name | Identification |
| LinkedIn URL | Quick access |
| Sequence Start Date | When you sent Touch 1 |
| Current Touch # | Where they are in the sequence |
| Last Message Sent Date | When you last messaged them |
| Next Touch Date | When to send the next message |
| Status | Active Sequence, Replied, Call Booked, Not Interested, Ghosted, Re-Engage Later |
| Reply Notes | Summary of any replies for context |
| Branch | Which branching logic you are following (A/B/C/D/E) |

### 5.2 Daily Sequence Management Routine

**Spend 15-20 minutes each morning:**
1. Check for new replies (handle immediately using branching logic above)
2. Review "Next Touch Date" column — who needs a follow-up today?
3. Send the appropriate touch for each prospect due today
4. Update the spreadsheet (current touch, next touch date, status, notes)
5. Add any new accepted connections to the sequence pipeline

### 5.3 Volume Management — Juggling Multiple Sequences

**Recommended concurrent sequences:**
- **Starting out:** 10-20 active sequences simultaneously
- **Experienced:** 30-50 active sequences simultaneously
- **With CRM/automation:** 50-100 active sequences

**Tip:** Stagger your sequence starts. If you start 20 sequences on the same day, you will have 20 Touch 2s due on the same day 3 days later. Instead, start 5-7 new sequences per day across 3-4 days for even distribution.

## Part 6: The Warm Sequence Variant

If a prospect has already engaged with your content (liked a post, commented, viewed your profile), use this modified sequence that acknowledges the existing warmth.

### Warm Sequence Modifications

| Touch | Standard Sequence | Warm Sequence Modification |
|---|---|---|
| Touch 1 | Generic opener framework | Reference their specific engagement: "Saw you liked my post about..." |
| Touch 2 | Value add (day 3-4) | Share content directly related to what they engaged with (day 2-3) |
| Touch 3 | Social proof (day 7-8) | Social proof + personalized tie-in to their engagement (day 5-6) |
| Touch 4 | Question (day 12-14) | Question directly related to their content interests (day 9-10) |
| Touch 5 | Direct ask (day 18-21) | Direct ask with reference to shared interests (day 14-16) |

**Key difference:** The warm sequence is compressed (shorter intervals) and more personalized, because you have more context to work with.

**Warm Touch 1 Example:**
```
Hi [Name], noticed you liked my post about [TOPIC] — glad it resonated. Your comment on [OTHER PERSON]'s post about [RELATED TOPIC] was really insightful too. Curious — is [CHALLENGE] something you're actively working on at [COMPANY]?
```
