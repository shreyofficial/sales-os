# Omnichannel Sequence Designer

## Purpose

Design coordinated LinkedIn + email outreach cadences that function as a single unified system. When a prospect encounters you across multiple channels — each touchpoint reinforcing the last — your reply rates multiply and your perceived authority skyrockets. This skill builds personalized omnichannel sequences tailored to your service, ICP, and sales cycle.

---

## Why Omnichannel Outperforms Single-Channel

### The Data

| Metric | Email Only | LinkedIn Only | Omnichannel (Email + LinkedIn) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average reply rate | 3-5% | 5-12% | 15-25% |
| Meetings booked per 100 prospects | 1-2 | 2-4 | 5-8 |
| Time to first reply | 4-7 days | 2-4 days | 1-3 days |
| Cost per meeting | High | Medium | Low |

### The Psychology Behind It

1. **The Mere Exposure Effect** — Prospects who see your name across two or more channels develop unconscious familiarity. By the time you ask for a call, you feel like someone they "already know."
2. **Channel Credibility Stacking** — An email alone can feel like spam. A LinkedIn connection alone can feel random. But when a prospect receives a thoughtful email AND sees your insightful LinkedIn activity AND gets a personalized DM — you become a real, credible human with something to offer.
3. **Multiple Entry Points** — Some people live in email. Others check LinkedIn first thing in the morning. Omnichannel meets prospects where they already are, instead of hoping they check the one channel you chose.
4. **The "Surround Sound" Perception** — When done correctly, the prospect feels like you're "everywhere" in their world — not because you're stalking them, but because your presence signals relevance and authority in their space.

### The Critical Rule: Coordination, Not Repetition

Omnichannel does NOT mean sending the same message on two channels. Each channel carries a distinct angle, tone, and value proposition. The prospect should never feel like they received the same outreach twice. Instead, each touchpoint builds on the last and introduces something new.

---

## The "Surround Sound" Strategy

### Core Principles

1. **Stagger, don't stack.** Never hit both channels on the same day (except Day 1 email + Day 1 LinkedIn connection, which are different enough in format).
2. **Vary the angle.** If your email leads with a pain point, your LinkedIn DM leads with curiosity or value. If your email shares a case study, your LinkedIn comment adds a fresh insight.
3. **Respect the channel's native tone.** LinkedIn messages are shorter, more conversational, and more personal. Emails can be slightly longer and more structured. Don't write a LinkedIn DM that reads like an email.
4. **Let engagement on one channel inform the other.** If they opened your email three times, your next LinkedIn DM can be more direct. If they accepted your connection instantly, your next email can be warmer.
5. **One conversation, two channels.** The prospect should feel like they are in a single, coherent conversation with you — not receiving disconnected blasts from two systems.

### Channel Strengths

| Channel | Best For | Tone | Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn Connection Request | First impression, establishing mutual relevance | Casual, peer-to-peer | 1-2 sentences (under 300 characters with note) |
| LinkedIn DM | Relationship building, quick questions, voice notes | Conversational, friendly | 2-4 sentences |
| LinkedIn Content Engagement | Building visibility, demonstrating expertise | Thoughtful, value-adding | 2-3 sentence comments |
| Email | Detailed value props, case studies, resource sharing | Professional but human | 50-120 words |

## The 14-Day Omnichannel Sequence Framework

### Day 1: LinkedIn Connection Request (with personalized note)

**Objective:** Get in their world. Establish the first touchpoint on a personal, peer-to-peer platform.

**Best Practices:**
- Reference something specific: their content, a mutual connection, a company milestone, or a shared group
- Do NOT pitch in the connection request
- Keep it under 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit for connection notes)
- Send between 8-10 AM in the prospect's time zone (highest acceptance rates)

**Template:**

```
Hi [FIRST NAME] — saw your [SPECIFIC POST/COMMENT/ARTICLE] about [TOPIC].
Really resonated with your take on [SPECIFIC POINT]. Would love to connect.
```

**Alternate Template (Mutual Connection):**

```
Hi [FIRST NAME] — noticed we're both connected to [MUTUAL CONNECTION].
I work in [YOUR SPACE] and love following conversations in [THEIR INDUSTRY].
Let's connect.
```

**Alternate Template (Company Trigger):**

```
Hi [FIRST NAME] — congrats on [COMPANY MILESTONE: funding round, product launch,
new hire announcement]. Exciting stuff. Would love to follow along.
```

**If they've already responded on another channel:** Skip this step — you're already connected through conversation. Send a connection request without a note, or with a brief "Great chatting via email — let's connect here too."

---

### Day 2: Email 1 — Cold Email Opener (Different Angle Than LinkedIn)

**Objective:** Introduce yourself through a different lens. If your LinkedIn note was about their content, your email is about their business challenge. Different channel, different angle, same person.

**Best Practices:**
- Subject line must be short, specific, and curiosity-inducing (4-7 words)
- Do NOT reference your LinkedIn request (you want this to feel like an independent touchpoint — the "surround sound" effect)
- Lead with their problem, not your solution
- One clear CTA: a question, not a meeting request

**Template:**

```
Subject: [PAIN POINT] at [COMPANY]?

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Most [ICP TITLE]s I talk to at [COMPANY SIZE/TYPE] companies are dealing
with [SPECIFIC PAIN POINT] — especially when [SITUATIONAL CONTEXT that
makes it timely].

We've helped [NUMBER] [SIMILAR COMPANIES/ROLES] [SPECIFIC OUTCOME] in
[TIMEFRAME] by [HIGH-LEVEL APPROACH — not a feature dump].

Curious — is [PAIN POINT] something on your radar right now?

[YOUR NAME]
```

**What to do if they accepted your LinkedIn connection already:** Great signal. Continue with the email as planned — but mentally note them as a warmer lead. Your Day 6 LinkedIn DM can reference both touchpoints.

---

### Day 3: LinkedIn — Engage With Their Content

**Objective:** Build familiarity without direct outreach. Show up in their notifications as someone who adds value, not someone who wants something.

**Best Practices:**
- Find a recent post they published (within the last 7 days)
- Leave a substantive comment (not "Great post!") — add your perspective, ask a follow-up question, share a related data point
- Like the post as well
- If they haven't posted recently, engage with something they've commented on, or like 2-3 of their older posts to trigger a notification
- Spend 3-5 minutes per prospect on this step — quality matters

**Comment Template:**

```
This is a great point, [FIRST NAME]. I've seen this play out especially
with [SPECIFIC EXAMPLE FROM YOUR EXPERIENCE]. The part about [THEIR
SPECIFIC POINT] is underrated — in my experience, [YOUR ADDITIONAL
INSIGHT OR QUESTION]. Have you found that [FOLLOW-UP QUESTION]?
```

**If they have no content to engage with:** Like their most recent activity (a comment, a share) or skip to a DM on Day 6 and double up the value there.

**If they already replied to your email:** Do NOT skip this step. Content engagement is low-pressure and additive. It reinforces that you're a real person in their space.

---

### Day 5: Email 2 — Value Add / Resource Share

**Objective:** Give before you ask. Share something genuinely useful that positions you as an expert without pitching.

**Best Practices:**
- Share a resource that's relevant to the pain point from Email 1: a blog post, case study, industry report, short video, framework, or checklist
- The resource should be something you created OR curated — either works
- Keep the email short; let the resource do the heavy lifting
- Do NOT ask for a meeting yet

**Template:**

```
Subject: Re: [ORIGINAL SUBJECT LINE] (reply thread to Email 1 if possible)

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thought you might find this useful — we put together a [RESOURCE TYPE]
on [TOPIC RELATED TO THEIR PAIN POINT].

[LINK or ATTACHMENT]

The key takeaway: [ONE SENTENCE SUMMARY of the most valuable insight].

Figured it was relevant given [REFERENCE TO THEIR SITUATION — company
stage, role, industry trend].

Worth a look if [PAIN POINT] is on your plate.

[YOUR NAME]
```

**If they replied to Email 1:** Stop the automated sequence. Respond to their reply directly. If the reply was positive, move to call booking. If neutral ("interesting, tell me more"), send the resource as part of your personal reply.

---

### Day 6: LinkedIn DM (If Connected) or Voice Note (If Not)

**Objective:** First direct LinkedIn message. This is where the omnichannel effect kicks in — they've seen your name in their inbox AND on LinkedIn. Now you reach out personally.

**If Connected — DM Template:**

```
Hey [FIRST NAME] — appreciate the connection. I noticed [SPECIFIC
OBSERVATION: something from their profile, a post they made, their
company's recent news].

Quick question — are you and your team currently [QUESTION RELATED TO
YOUR SERVICE AREA]? I've been working with a few [ICP TITLE]s on
[TOPIC] and curious if it's top of mind for you too.
```

**If NOT Connected — Voice Note Strategy:**

LinkedIn voice notes have extremely high open and response rates (up to 30%). If your connection request hasn't been accepted:

1. Send a new connection request (some expire or get buried)
2. If you have LinkedIn Premium / Sales Navigator, send an InMail with a voice note
3. Alternatively, send a brief InMail text referencing your email: "Hey [FIRST NAME] — I sent you a quick email earlier this week about [TOPIC]. Thought I'd reach out here too since I know inboxes get buried. Would love to connect."

**If they replied to your email positively:** Pivot this DM to a warm reinforcement: "Hey [FIRST NAME] — great chatting over email. Thought I'd connect here too so we can stay in touch. Looking forward to [NEXT STEP from email conversation]."

---

### Day 8: Email 3 — Social Proof / Case Study

**Objective:** Reduce risk and build trust with evidence. Show that people like them have achieved results like the ones you're implying.

**Best Practices:**
- Choose a case study or testimonial from a client in the same industry, company size, or role as the prospect
- Be specific with numbers: percentage improvements, dollar amounts, time savings
- Frame it as a story, not a pitch
- Include a soft CTA: "Would it make sense to explore if something similar could work for [COMPANY]?"

**Template:**

```
Subject: How [SIMILAR COMPANY/ROLE] [ACHIEVED SPECIFIC RESULT]

Hi [FIRST NAME],

Thought this might be relevant — [CLIENT DESCRIPTION: similar company
type, role, or industry] was dealing with [THE SAME PAIN POINT you
identified in Email 1].

They were [BEFORE STATE: specific, painful situation]. After working
together for [TIMEFRAME], they [AFTER STATE: specific, measurable
result].

The biggest shift was [KEY INSIGHT — what changed for them that was
non-obvious].

Would it make sense to explore if something similar could work for
[COMPANY]?

[YOUR NAME]

P.S. Happy to share the full breakdown if you're interested.
```

**If they opened Email 2 but didn't reply:** Good engagement signal. This email should land well — social proof builds on the value you already shared. Consider making the subject line a reply thread to increase open rates.

---

### Day 10: LinkedIn — Engage With Another Post + DM Follow-Up

**Objective:** Double touchpoint day on LinkedIn. Re-engage with their content and follow up on your earlier DM.

**Step 1: Content Engagement**
- Find another recent post (or a post they've commented on) and leave a thoughtful comment
- This keeps you visible in their feed and notifications

**Step 2: DM Follow-Up (only if your Day 6 DM went unanswered)**

```
Hey [FIRST NAME] — no worries if my last message got buried. I actually
just saw your comment about [TOPIC] — really interesting take.

Reason I reached out: I've been helping [ICP TITLE]s at [COMPANY TYPE]
companies [ACHIEVE SPECIFIC OUTCOME], and based on what I've seen at
[COMPANY], it seems like there might be a fit.

Would you be open to a quick 15-min chat to see if it makes sense?
```

**If they replied to your Day 6 DM:** Skip the follow-up DM. Just do the content engagement. Respond to their DM reply naturally.

---

### Day 12: Email 4 — Different Angle / Question-Based

**Objective:** Approach the same prospect from a completely new angle. If previous emails focused on a specific pain point, this one asks a strategic question or introduces a different benefit.

**Template:**

```
Subject: Quick question about [DIFFERENT ASPECT OF THEIR BUSINESS]

Hi [FIRST NAME],

I've been thinking about [COMPANY]'s [DIFFERENT ANGLE: market position,
growth trajectory, team structure, competitive landscape].

Curious — how is your team currently handling [STRATEGIC QUESTION
related to your service but framed as a business question, not a
product question]?

Asking because a few [ICP TITLE]s I've spoken to recently mentioned
that [INSIGHT OR TREND that makes this question timely].

Would love to hear your perspective — even if just a one-line reply.

[YOUR NAME]
```

**Why a different angle?** By Day 12, if the prospect hasn't replied to pain-point-focused emails, a new angle might resonate. Maybe the original pain point isn't their top priority. This email explores a different entry point.

---

### Day 14: LinkedIn DM (Direct Ask) OR Email 5 (Breakup)

**Objective:** Final touchpoint. Make a direct, clear ask — or close the sequence gracefully.

**Option A: LinkedIn DM — Direct Ask for Call**

Use this if they've shown any engagement signals (accepted connection, viewed profile, opened emails, liked content).

```
Hey [FIRST NAME] — I know I've reached out a couple of times, so I
want to be respectful of your time.

I genuinely think there's a way to help [COMPANY] [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]
based on what I've seen working with similar [COMPANY TYPE] companies.

Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call this week to explore it?
No pressure — happy to share some ideas even if the timing isn't right.

[CALENDAR LINK or "What does your Tuesday/Thursday look like?"]
```

**Option B: Email 5 — The Breakup Email**

Use this if there has been zero engagement across all channels.

```
Subject: Should I close your file?

Hi [FIRST NAME],

I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back — which is totally
fine. I don't want to keep cluttering your inbox.

I'll assume the timing isn't right and close out your file for now. If
[PAIN POINT] becomes a priority down the road, my door is always open.

Wishing you and the [COMPANY] team all the best.

[YOUR NAME]
```

**Why breakup emails work:** They create a sense of loss aversion. When someone is told "I'm going away," they often re-engage. Breakup emails consistently generate 30-40% of all sequence replies.

## Channel Priority Rules

### When They Reply on LinkedIn

- **Move the conversation to LinkedIn.** Stop sending sequence emails immediately (or reduce to one final touchpoint).
- Continue all follow-up via DM.
- Only move to email if the conversation requires longer-form content (sending documents, proposals, calendar links) or if they explicitly suggest email.

### When They Reply to Email

- **Keep the conversation in email.** Pause LinkedIn DM outreach.
- You can still engage with their content on LinkedIn (low-pressure) but do not send DMs about business topics — keep it all in the email thread.
- If the email conversation stalls, you can re-open on LinkedIn.

### When They Reply on Both Channels

- **Let the prospect lead.** Whichever channel they seem most responsive on, prioritize that one.
- Consolidate: "Hey, I know we've been chatting in a couple of places — happy to keep the conversation here if that's easier."
- Do NOT have parallel business conversations on both channels simultaneously.

## Handling Cross-Channel Signals

### Signal: They Viewed Your LinkedIn Profile After Receiving Your Email

**What it means:** They're curious. They received your email, wanted to know who you are, and looked you up on LinkedIn. This is a strong buying signal.

**Action:** Accelerate your LinkedIn outreach. If your next LinkedIn touchpoint is 2 days away, move it to tomorrow. Send a DM that's slightly warmer and more direct. Example:

```
Hey [FIRST NAME] — noticed you checked out my profile. I'd love to
connect and swap ideas about [TOPIC]. What do you think?
```

### Signal: They Opened Your Email Multiple Times But Didn't Reply

**What it means:** They're interested but not compelled to act. The subject got their attention, the content held it, but the CTA didn't push them over the edge.

**Action:** Your next touchpoint (email or LinkedIn) should be more direct with a clearer, lower-friction CTA. Instead of "Would it make sense to chat?", try "I can send over a 2-minute video walking through how this would work for [COMPANY]. Want me to?"

### Signal: They Accepted Your Connection But Haven't Responded to DM

**What it means:** They're open to being in your network but not ready for a conversation. Don't over-interpret acceptance — some people accept everyone.

**Action:** Do NOT send another DM immediately. Instead, engage with their content for 3-5 days. Then send a follow-up DM that references your content engagement: "Loved your post about [TOPIC] the other day. It got me thinking about [RELATED INSIGHT]..."

### Signal: They Clicked a Link in Your Email

**What it means:** They actively engaged with your content. Strong interest signal.

**Action:** Your next email or DM should reference the resource: "I saw you checked out the [RESOURCE] — curious what stood out to you? Happy to dive deeper on any of it."

## Volume Management

### How Many Omnichannel Sequences to Run Simultaneously

| Your Availability | Active Sequences at Once | Daily Time Required |
|---|---|---|
| Part-time (sales is one of many roles) | 15-25 | 30-45 min/day |
| Dedicated outreach (sales-focused role) | 40-60 | 1-2 hours/day |
| Full-time sales team member | 60-100 | 2-3 hours/day |

### Why Less Is More

Running too many sequences leads to:
- Missed follow-ups (you forget where prospects are in the cadence)
- Generic messages (no time to personalize)
- Engagement signals you can't act on (you see they opened but have no bandwidth to adjust)

### Batching Strategy

- **Morning block (30 min):** Send all LinkedIn DMs and connection requests. Engage with content.
- **Midday block (30 min):** Send all emails. Review email analytics (opens, clicks, replies).
- **End of day (15 min):** Check for new engagement signals. Update your tracking sheet. Plan tomorrow's touchpoints.

## Tool Stack for Omnichannel Tracking

### Option 1: Spreadsheet-Based (Free)

Create a tracking spreadsheet with these columns:

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| Prospect Name | Full name |
| Company | Their company |
| LinkedIn URL | Profile link |
| Email Address | Primary email |
| Sequence Variant | Which of the 3 variants they're in |
| Current Day | Where they are in the 14-day sequence |
| Last Touchpoint | What was the last thing you sent and on which channel |
| Last Signal | Most recent engagement signal (open, click, view, reply) |
| Lead Score | From the Lead Scoring Framework |
| Next Action | What to do next and when |
| Status | Active / Replied / Booked / Paused / Completed |
| Notes | Any relevant context |

### Option 2: CRM-Based (Paid)

Use a CRM like HubSpot (free tier available), Pipedrive, or Close. Most CRMs support:
- Email sequence automation with tracking
- LinkedIn activity logging (manual, but structured)
- Pipeline stage management
- Engagement scoring

### Option 3: Purpose-Built Outreach Tools

- **Email sequencing:** Instantly, Lemlist, Smartlead, Apollo
- **LinkedIn automation:** Expandi, Dripify, PhantomBuster (use cautiously — LinkedIn TOS risk)
- **Combined:** Reply.io, Salesloft, Outreach

**Recommendation:** Start with the spreadsheet. Move to a CRM once you're running 30+ active sequences. Only adopt purpose-built tools when you've validated your messaging and are ready to scale.

## Three Complete Omnichannel Sequence Variants

### Variant 1: Aggressive (Sales-Heavy)

**Best for:** Short sales cycles, transactional services, time-sensitive offers, prospects in competitive markets where speed wins.

| Day | Channel | Action | Template Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LinkedIn | Connection request with note referencing a pain point | Direct, problem-aware |
| 1 | Email | Cold email with strong pain point lead + clear CTA | "Are you dealing with X?" |
| 3 | LinkedIn | DM (if connected) with case study link | Social proof, urgency |
| 4 | Email | Follow-up with ROI calculator or quantified benefit | Numbers-driven |
| 6 | LinkedIn | DM with direct ask for call | "15 min this week?" |
| 7 | Email | Objection-handling email (preempt common concerns) | "Most people worry about X..." |
| 9 | LinkedIn | Voice note (personal, direct) | Humanize, stand out |
| 10 | Email | Breakup email | Scarcity, loss aversion |

**Total touchpoints:** 8 in 10 days. High intensity.

### Variant 2: Relationship (Value-Heavy)

**Best for:** High-ticket services, long sales cycles, trust-dependent industries (consulting, financial services, healthcare), senior-level prospects.

| Day | Channel | Action | Template Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | LinkedIn | Connection request — genuine, no pitch | Shared interest, mutual connection |
| 3 | LinkedIn | Engage with their content (thoughtful comment) | Build visibility |
| 5 | Email | Value-first email — share a resource, no ask | "Thought you'd find this useful" |
| 7 | LinkedIn | Like + comment on another post | Stay visible |
| 9 | LinkedIn | DM — reference their content + ask a question | Curiosity, not pitching |
| 11 | Email | Case study + soft CTA | "Would it make sense to explore?" |
| 14 | LinkedIn | DM — personal note + meeting request | Warm, earned ask |
| 17 | Email | Follow-up or breakup | Respectful close |

**Total touchpoints:** 8 in 17 days. Low pressure, high trust.

### Variant 3: Hybrid (Balanced)

**Best for:** Most B2B services, mid-market prospects, standard sales cycles.

This is the 14-day framework detailed above. It balances directness with value-giving, uses both channels roughly equally, and escalates gradually from awareness to ask.

**Total touchpoints:** 9 in 14 days. Balanced intensity.

## Quality Checklist

Before launching your omnichannel sequences, verify:

- [ ] Each LinkedIn and email touchpoint has a DIFFERENT angle (no copy-paste across channels)
- [ ] LinkedIn messages are short, conversational, and platform-native (not email-style)
- [ ] Emails follow cold email best practices: short subject lines, 50-120 words, single CTA
- [ ] You have a clear escalation path: awareness > value > proof > ask
- [ ] Channel priority rules are documented: what happens when they reply on each channel
- [ ] You have a tracking system set up (spreadsheet, CRM, or tool) before sending the first message
- [ ] Volume is manageable: you can personally follow up on engagement signals within 24 hours
- [ ] Every touchpoint passes the "Would I find this annoying?" test — if yes, rewrite it
- [ ] Breakup emails and final DMs are respectful and leave the door open
- [ ] All personalization tokens ([FIRST NAME], [COMPANY], [PAIN POINT]) are filled in — no placeholders left
- [ ] You've reviewed the full sequence from the prospect's perspective: read every touchpoint in order and ask "Does this feel like a coherent, value-adding conversation?"
- [ ] You have at least one alternate message for each touchpoint in case A/B testing reveals a weakness
